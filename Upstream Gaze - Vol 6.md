PUBLISHER’S NOTE 
T
he new year offers a renewed opportunity to map challenges and 
plot strategic directions to address the multifaceted issues that 
have constrain Nigeria from achieving its goals with regard to 
the upstream sector. This edition of the Upstream Gaze outlines 
strategic regulatory directions captured in the NUPRC REGULATORY 
ACTION PLAN (RAP), an imperative document designed in compliance 
with the Petroleum Industry Act and the Renewed Hope agenda of 
President Bola Tinubu.
The action plan seeks to reverse the recent trend of investor apathy in 
Nigeria by addressing the prominent challenges namely unpredictability in 
acreage availability through licencing rounds, high cost of development and 
production, regulatory uncertainty (pre-PIA era), disruptions in operations 
due to community conflicts, security challenges, contractual bottlenecks 
and related social issues. Similarly, investor apathy has further been exac-
erbated by a global transition from fossil fuels such as oil and gas.
As such, the action plan outlines robust solutions such as effective 
regulatory initiatives, ease of administration, Industry engagements, 
performance evaluation among others. This edition promises to imbue 
stakeholders with a comprehensive understanding of the NUPRC’s reg-
ulatory direction for the year 2024
 
Happy reading!

Back in October 2016, the Federal Government 
launched the ‘7-Big Wins Agenda’. The major objective of 
the agenda was to create a stable and enabling environ-
ment, and the expectation was that it would maximise 
investment opportunities in the oil and gas sector

Other reforms in the sector followed as the govern-

oil-based to a gas-based economy by developing gas 
infrastructure, commercialising gas flare, implementing 
a gas commercial framework and maximising the use of 
gas to power economic development. It also included 
some tax and regulatory developments in the sector.
The promulgation of the Petroleum Industry Act (PIA) 
in August 2021 signalled a comprehensive overhaul 
of the oil and gas sector in Nigeria. The purpose is 
to provide legal, governance, regulatory and fiscal 
framework for the Nigerian petroleum industry. The 
PIA (2021) has been variously seen as one of the most 
audacious attempts to overhaul the oil and gas sector in 
Nigeria. A lot has already been done in this direction by 
the Nigerian Upstream Regulatory Commission (NUPRC) 
and other sister agencies. The Act has been designed 
to help attract and create investment opportunities for 
local and international investors.
It is in furtherance of this objective that on March 
6, 2024, His Excellency President Bola Ahmed Tinubu 
introduced three policy directives as part of an ongoing 
effort to create a more enabling environment conducive 
to strategic investment in the oil and gas sector.

and inclusive government policies is the most enduring 
path to prosperity for all Nigerians. He is therefore 
committed to sustained engagement and collaboration 
with key investors to ensure improvement in the ease of 
doing business in Nigeria.
The President is determined to remove obstacles to 
investments in Nigeria, improve the investment climate, 
position Nigeria as the preferred investment destina-
tion for the oil and gas sector in Africa and diversify the 
economy for the benefit of all Nigerians. It is obvious 
that the country has been facing a revenue crisis over 
the years. The situation was further compounded by 
oil theft, low production levels, price volatility in the 
international market, aging infrastructure and funding 
constraints. Something strategic had to be done quickly 
to stem the tide. President Tinubu decided to grab the 
bull by the horns. The objective is to grow revenue and 
use it to enable diverse economic opportunities for 
Nigerians. 
Just as the NUPRC Chief Executive, Engr. Gbenga 
Komolafe has always pointed out, Nigeria has a revenue 
challenge in spite of its natural resources, and this is as 
a result of the various setbacks in the exploitation and 
processing corridors. The resultant revenue crisis faced 
by the government impacts all Nigerians. To urgently 
address this, President Bola Tinubu is actively seeking 
ways to grow revenue and forex to stabilise the country’s 
economy and the currency. The oil and gas sector is 
critical to our ability to do so. The President’s Special 
Assistant on Energy,  Olu Verhejen, explained. “However, 
our current oil and gas production and investment levels 
fall significantly short of our potential.”
It is noteworthy that since 2016, Nigeria has accounted 
for only four percent (4%) of Africa's total oil and gas 
investments, despite possessing thirty-eight percent 
(38%) of the continent's hydrocarbon reserves. So, there 
was an urgent need to push the frontiers and walk the 
trajectory that would see the oil and gas industry play 
its expected role in energising the country’s economy.

fiscal incentives, reduce contracting and project timelines 
and promote cost efficiency for oil and gas projects. Also, 
given the urgency to accelerate investments in this 
sector to stabilise the economy, the directives, which

The window for attracting new investments and 
exploring our vast reserves is fast narrowing. If the 
global energy transition accelerates, approximately 60% 
of Nigeria’s reserves could be uncompetitive to produce. 
Against this backdrop, we have identified that there are 
so many licenses with proven reserves that are not being 
optimized in the hands of IOCs, NOC, and others. In line 
with Mr. President’s Renewed Hope agenda, we are 
working on changing this narrative. 
One of the easiest ways to solve the current foreign 
exchange challenges faced in the country is to aggres-

additional revenue for the government. The additional 
crude produced will serve as a feedstock to our state-
owned, private, and modular refineries. Thereby changing 
the narrative of Nigeria being a major refined petroleum

is NOW! We need to take bold and decisive actions to 
harness the vast hydrocarbon resources.
Earlier this year I attended the World Economic Forum 
in Davos, where I participated in about nine panels. All 
these panels shared the same message; “Stop investment 
in fossil fuels”. But why should we stop? No country is 
slowing down fossil fuel exploration and production. 
Recall that only recently, the UK government awarded 
over 100 licenses for exploration in the North Sea; fur-
thermore, the United States of America being the highest 
producer of oil today is also ramping up production. No 
country is reducing production.
As a government, ours is to provide a business-friendly 
and enabling environment for potential investors. This is 
what we are doing, and we are resolute in removing all 
bottlenecks, disincentives, and impediments preventing 
inflows of such investment. This is the whole essence of 
the PIA 2021.
As we acknowledge that additional financing is crucial 
for growth in the oil and gas industry, it is imperative 
that we pursue the dream of realizing the Africa Energy 
Bank, which will have an initial capital base of US$ 5 Billion

120 Billion by 2028.
We are taking a multi-faceted approach to boost the 
refining capacity in Nigeria as part of the ways to address 
the challenges associated with energy security. These ini-
tiatives aim to reduce dependency on imported refined 
products, reduce the pressure on foreign exchange, and 
enhance the country's ability to meet its domestic energy

The transition to cleaner and more sustainable energy 
sources is inevitable, and we are actively pursuing initia-
tives to position Nigeria as a leader in this energy transition. 
As we navigate this change, Nigeria recognizes the need 
to strike a balance between meeting our growing energy 
demand and reducing our carbon footprint. The diver-

energy, and the adoption of cleaner technologies are all 
integral components of our strategy. 
I am not oblivious that there are concerns around the 
divestments by some IOCs. However, this is a win-win

Our commitment to enhancing our crude oil reserves

POTENTIALS
Distinguished Ladies and Gentlemen, since its inception 
in October 2021, the Commission has been working assid-
uously to ensure that the PIA is effectively implemented 
for growth in oil and gas reserves as well as achieving the 
national average daily production target set at 2.5 million 
barrels of oil and condensate per day in the near term. The 
oil and gas reserves in Nigeria represents 30% and 34% 
of the African oil and gas reserves respectively. Our vast 
reserves hold immense potential for sustainable develop-
ment and prosperity.  
Although the actual national production currently 
averages 1.33 Million barrels of oil per day and 256 
thousand barrels of condensate per day, the national 
technical production potential currently stands at 2.26 
Million bpd while the current OPEC quota is 1.5 Million 
bopd. Closing the gap between the actual oil produc-
tion and the technical potential presents a window of 
investment opportunities for investors and a significant 
opportunity for Nigeria to unlock additional revenue 
streams, address the current foreign exchange gap and 
strengthen her economic resilience. So there is opportu-
nity in every disability. 
Aside from hydrocarbon resources, Nigeria is blessed 
with potentials for green and blue hydrogen, solar, wind, 
biomass and critical minerals for development of clean 
energy technologies as well as growing population pre-
dominated by young people. With a coastline along the 
Gulf of Guinea, market size of more than 200 million 
people projected to reach between 390 million and 440 
million people in 2050, Nigeria represents hope for Africa. 
Interestingly, about 70% of the Nigerian population are 
under 30, and 42% are under the age of 15 [Source: National 
Population Commission] representing a huge economic 
asset. Indeed, Nigeria’s potentials are derived from its 
human, natural and material resources which must be 
mobilised to propel her on a path of economic growth and 
development and for her sustainable energy future. 
As a country, Nigeria is also on track to meet its environ-
mental stewardship obligations. Analysis of EU’s EDGAR 
database shows that Nigeria occupies a distant 165th 
position in the global ranking of top emitters, with a GHG 
emissions per capita at 1.88 tonnes CO2 equivalent per 
capita per year, representing just 0.13% of the global total.

OTHER INVESTMENT OPPORTUNITIES 
Distinguished Participants, the Commission's proactive 
initiatives and drive in promoting investment have indeed 
opened a myriad of opportunities for investors in the oil 
and gas sector. One good example is the efforts in attract-
ing investors to boost investment in gas and support the 
Federal Government’s Decade of Gas Programme. At the 
heart of this programme is the vision to ensure that gas 
plays a key role to lift the nation from the challenges that 
confront her in order to drive sustainable development. 
Recent study by the Commission revealed that growth in 
gas demand outstrips supply. Between 2020 and 2030, 
demand is expected to grow exponentially at a compound 
annual growth rate of 16.6% p.a.  
Natural gas production is projected to increase from 8.0 
bcfd in 2020 to 12.2 bcfd in 2030 driven by major projects 
such as NLNG Train 7 & Train 8, Nigeria/Morocco pipeline, 
Ajaokuta-Kaduna-Kano (AKK) Natural Gas Pipeline Project, 
and so many other gas projects. Thus, the Commission is 
proactively pivoting the industry to close the projected 
supply gap by taking deliberate steps to drive the Decade 
of Gas Programme through a more aggressive devel-
opment of the nation’s huge gas resources, enhanced 
exploration activities, development of utilization schemes 
leading to gas reserves growth, increased gas production, 
maturation of domestic and export gas markets, in addition 
to gas flare elimination and commercialisation through the 
Nigeria Gas Flare Commercialization Programme (NGFCP). 
The import of this is to showcase the huge opportunities 
in gas business for both the existing investors and new 
entrants in the Nigerian upstream gas sector.  
Distinguished Ladies and Gentlemen, the Commission 
has created further investment opportunities through the 
ongoing licensing round for seven deepwater acreages as 
well as the proposed 2024 closed bids expected to increase 
the nation’s reserve and production and boost national 
revenue. At this point, permit me to reiterate that both 
open and closed bids are permissible by the law as Section 
73(1) of the PIA does not preclude either approach, so far as 
the licensing round is “…based on a fair, transparent and 
competitive bidding process”. In addition, huge opportu-
nities also exist in Seismic acquisition on multiclient basis, 
development of deeper hydrocarbon opportunities, drilling 
and well services in both green and several mature fields, 
waste management etc. There are also business prospects 
in decarbonisation and carbon-pricing system currently 
being stewarded by the Commission. Each of these areas 
provides a unique entry point for willing investors.

F.  Vacating Entry Barrier Associated with Huge 
      Asset Acquisition Fees
The Commission has paid close attention to the 
impact of high asset acquisition fees to the investment 
attractiveness of the upstream sector.   A careful review 
of regional Signature Bonuses across the globe based 
on Welligence Energy Analytics (WEA)evaluation 
revealed that several Host Governments are dialling 
back from the traditional hefty signature bonuses 
paid for exploration blocks. That review showed that 
Latin America has been the most active region where 
signature bonuses paid for exploration blocks have 
been disclosed. In Brazil, for instance, where multiple 
license rounds have been organised in the last decade, 
Signature bonuses paid for deepwater blocks dropped 
from over US$ 6 billion paid for exploration blocks 
in 2013 to an average of US$43 million in the round 
of 2022”. The WEA’s review recognised Guyana basin 
as one of the highly sought-after exploration basins 
globally and noted that in recent license round the 
minimum signature bonus was US$10 million and 
US$20 million for shallow water and deepwater blocks 
respectively. 
Similarly, signature bonuses in regions like Middle 
East (e.g. Israel) and North Africa, where blocks have 
been awarded recently, are estimated to be around 
US$10 million. In South-East Asia, for instance in 
Indonesia, signature bonuses are in the region of US$1 
to $1.5 million while in Thailand’s recent bid round, 
bidders were expected to offer at least US$3 million 
signature bonus.  In Sub-Sahara Africa, on the other 
hand, signature bonuses paid for exploration blocks 
awarded in recent years has not been disclosed, “but 
license bid rounds in Angola and Nigeria have attracted 
hefty signature bonuses in the past”, according to WEA. 
For example, the Angolan Block, 15/06, raked in more 
than US$900 million in signature bonus in April 2006, 
as reported by Energy Intelligence, 2006. 
The import of the above narrative is to showcase 
the current disposition of several regional host 
governments towards entry fees such as signature 
bonuses which has a huge implication on the direction 
of flow of investments. 
Recognising that the era of front-loaded huge 
signature bonuses is over, the NUPRC, in the ongoing 
deep offshore licensing round, has become a lot more 
pragmatic in ensuring that entry fees do not become 
a barrier to entry for investment in exploration blocks 
offered. As a responsible regulator, the Commission will 
continue to review the prevailing global investment 
climate to ensure that the entry fees associated with all 
future licensing rounds are competitive in the context 
of global realities and energy transition imperatives and 
advise government accordingly.

Global crude oil prices have fluctuated significantly, 
with key factors influencing contemporary oil and gas 
pricing including but not limited to transition to cleaner 
energy, geopolitical tensions and supply concerns,

technological advancements and innovation, energy 
transition and investment shifts, regulatory changes & 
carbon pricing, supply chain challenges etc. Prices can 
vary from below $30 per barrel to well over $100 per

supply-demand dynamics, weather patterns affecting 
consumption, production levels, storage capacities, 
and global energy policies. Prices can also fluctuate 
substantially, moving between different price bands, 
affected by seasonal changes and unforeseen events, 
including regional or global geopolitics.

of the Seventh Schedule of the PIA, the Commission has 
established an Oil and Gas Pricing and Value Monitoring 
Desk to study trends and assumptions and advise in 
tandem with market realities. The Desk will carry on 
market analysis and forecasts that will avert undue 
exposure to Nigeria and industry stakeholders.

A BRIEF INTRODUCTION
The award of new leases and renewal of existing leases 
attracts a financial obligation called the signature and 
renewal bonuses, respectively. These obligations are, by 
law, a percentage of the value of the asset considered. 
This introduction to the basic concepts informs Industry 
approach to asset valuation and is intended to provide 
some insight and basis for future conversations on the 
topic. 
The Funnel & Filters model (Fig. 1) presented by 
Kodukula et al. (2006) represents how a portfolio of 
projects works. Each project's end-to-end life cycle 
comprises three phases: Assessment, Development, 
and Production. In the assessment phase, project 
ideas are initiated and assessed for their merit and 
how they compare with other competing ideas. The 
ideas go through a filter(s), which helps select those 
deemed worthwhile for investment and reject others. 
The chosen ideas become "formal" projects and enter 
the next phase. The objective of the development 
phase is to develop the target product (or service). In 
this phase, additional filters facilitate management's 
contingent decisions (defer investment; abandon, 
expand, contract, or continue the project at the planned 
scale; etc.) on the product development. The end of 
this phase is marked by the "launch" of the product 
associated with the project.

Practitioners consider project valuation the most 
critical part of the selection process because it assigns a 
dollar value to the project. In a broad sense, the project 
value is the net difference between the project revenues 
and costs over its entire life cycle. If the project's net 
revenues during the production phase are higher than 
the investment costs, the project is considered worthy 
of investment. Several tools are available to calculate the 
value of the project, and the quality of the valuation is 
related to the validity of the tools and how effectively 
they account for three essential factors:
1.	
Cash flow streams - (investment costs and 
production phase costs and revenues) through the 
entire project life cycle.
2.	
The Discount rate considered - is used to discount 
future cash flows to account for their uncertainty.
3.	
Availability 
of 
management's 
contingent 
decisions to change the course of the project.
Project cash flows represent both the benefits and 
costs of the project. The estimate of the project cash flows 
is for the project life cycle, including the development 
and production phases. 
There are two cash flow streams: 
1.	
Investment cost.
2.	
Net revenues in the production phase. 
The investment cash flow stream comprises the 
development and production phase capital costs. The 
"free cash flow" is the cash flow after depreciation, 
taxes, working capital, etc., are deducted from the 
revenue. The crux of project valuation lies in estimating 
these two cash flow streams over the entire project life 
cycle and discounting them back to today's value using 
the appropriate discount rate.
A discount rate refers to the rate used to convert the 
future value of the project cash flows to today's value. 
It is adjusted for the risk associated with the project, 
the higher the risk, the higher the discount rate. The 
uncertainty of the cash flows dictates the project risk.
Above notwithstanding, most projects involve 
contingent decisions, where senior management can 
change the course of the project by deciding whether 
to defer the investment for a while, abandon, expand 
or contract the project, maintain the status quo, and so 
on. Great strategic value, which can be advantageous, is 
embedded in these decisions. However, management 
must recognize it and be willing to exercise those 
decisions. Recommended practice is to acknowledge the 
contribution of contingent decisions in estimating the 
actual value of a project in the assessment phase. 
Three traditional valuation tools incorporate some or all 
of the factors discussed above in calculating project value:
1.	
Discounted cash flow (DCF) analysis.
2.	
Monte Carlo simulation. 
3.	
Decision trees. 
Irrespective of the tool, the building blocks for the 
calculations are provided by present values of the cash 
flow streams. Any valuation starts with estimation 
of development and production phase costs and net 
revenues (free cash flows) over the project life. Because 
of the time value of money, each cash flow from the future 
is converted into today's dollars, using the formula:
PV = FV/(1+r) n
Where FV is the future value, PV is the present value, r 
is the discount rate per time period, and n is the number 
of the time period. For convenience, 1/ (1 + r) n is typically 
calculated as the "discount factor" for a given discount 
rate and time period and is multiplied by the FV to arrive 
at the corresponding PV.
The principle of PV applies to every project valuation 
method. It is fundamental to any valuation tool, 
traditional tools, and more advanced techniques 
involving real options.

ACCOUNTING FOR RISK
In the finance world, risks are broadly classified as market 
risks and private risks. Although experts differ in the 
exact definition of these risks, it is generally accepted 
that risks that can be captured in the value of a traded 
security are market risks, and all the others are private 
risks (Amram and Kulatilaka, 2000). For example, oil 
price fluctuations are market risks because they are 
captured in the value of oil futures contracts traded in 
financial markets. 
In a broad sense, a practitioner can consider the 
market risk of a project to be due to the volatility of its 
expected future payoff (the net cash flow) driven by the 
market forces, such as market demand, competition, 
and so on. The private risks are related to the efficiency 
of an organisation in completing the project and the 
effectiveness of the technology related to the project. 
Kodukula et al. (2006) provided two reasons why it is 
essential to recognize and differentiate private risks from 
market risks in project valuation:
1.	
Financial option pricing models are based on the 
premise that a traded security captures the market 
risk of an underlying asset.
2.	
Investors are willing to pay a risk premium for 
market-driven rather than private risks. Thus, at a 
more practical level, the rate used to discount the 
cash flows is dictated by the risk associated with a 
given cash flow. 
3.	
Every project valuation method is in some way 
built on net present value (NPV) calculations, and 
every NPV calculation requires a discount rate. A 
practitioner's most significant dilemma is what 
discount rate to use in the NPV calculations. Two 
important factors determine the discount rate for a 
given cash flow stream:
4.	
The magnitude of risk (cash flow uncertainty) 
5.	
Type of risk (private risk versus market risk)
Regardless of the risk influencing the cash flow, no 
uncertainty means no risk; therefore, an investor will not 
pay a risk premium. Where there is no uncertainty on a 
cash flow stream, the appropriate discount rate is risk-
free. If uncertainty is associated with a cash flow stream, 
the next consideration is the type of risk influencing it. 
In cases where the risk is private, the investor will not 
pay a risk premium for the ineptness of the organisation 
in completing the project or the ineffectiveness of the 
technology involved. On the other hand, if the cash flow 
is affected by market risk, it is accounted for in some 
fashion - most commonly by adjusting the discount rate. 
There needs to be consensus in the finance community 
on determining the appropriate discount rate for a given 
cash flow stream and the valuation method.
LIMITATIONS OF TRADITIONAL 
VALUATION TOOLS
In a typical evaluation, analysts use sensitivity analysis 
to identify the key uncertainties associated with a 
project, assess probabilities for these uncertainties, and 
construct decision trees or simulation models of the 
project cash flows. They use these models to calculate 
expected net present values (NPVs) and distributions on 
NPVs, essential inputs into the decision-making process.
Though the practice is mainly satisfactory to decision-
makers, there are two significant concerns. Analyses 
only sometimes capture the flexibilities associated with 
projects, and decision models typically assume that 
management makes an initial investment decision. Then 
the project uncertainties are resolved, and cash flows are 
determined. In reality, the organisation makes a series of 
investment decisions as the uncertainties resolve over 
time. For example, when considering the development of 
a new oil field, if oil prices, production rates, or reserves 
exceed their expectations or if production technology 
improves, the firm can develop more aggressively or 
expand to nearby fields. Similarly, if prices, rates, or 
reserves are below expectations, the firm can scale back 
planned investments and limit its downside exposure. 
A second issue relates to how cash flow is discounted. 
NPVs for investments with long-term horizons are 
known to be extremely sensitive to the discount rate 
used. For these projects, the NPVs reflect the cost of 
capital and desired rate of return. Practitioners in the 
industry's exploration and new venture parts have 
expressed concern that the blanket use of a "risk-
adjusted" discount rate in calculating NPVs causes them 
to undervalue projects with long time horizons.

CONCLUSION
A fiscal system can be assessed in terms of its impact 
on investment decisions in either the short run (capital 
allocation within an existing portfolio of assets) or the 
long run (the decision to reject or invest in a project), in 
other words, by its neutrality. This can be expressed in 
terms of the net present value (NPV) of the expected 
project cash flows. Intuitively:
1.	
All taxes reduce the NPV of a project and make it less 
attractive. Therefore, the higher the level of taxation, 
the lower the number of possible investments under 
prevailing market conditions.
2.	
The timing of revenue collection is a major 
determinant of the NPV of a project. Fiscal systems 
that reduce or defer revenue collection (that is, 
are back-end loaded) are preferred by companies 
because they increase the NPV and accelerate the 
investment’s payback.
3.	
The NPV is significantly influenced by the risk 
profile of the investment. Therefore, fiscal systems 
that reduce the perceived political or economic 
risks are preferred.

1.2   THE CONFERENCE OF PARTIES (COP) 
All States that are Parties to the UNFCCC are rep-
resented at the COP, at which they review the 
implementation of the Convention and any other legal 
instruments that the COP adopts and take decisions 
necessary to promote the effective implementation of 
the Convention, including institutional and administra-
tive arrangements. 
A key task for the COP was to review the national 
communications and emission inventories submitted 
by Parties. Based on this information, the COP assesses 
the effects of the measures taken by Parties and the 
progress made in achieving the ultimate objective of 
the Convention. 
The COP meets every year unless the Parties decide 
otherwise. The first COP meeting was held in Berlin, 
Germany in March 1995. Just as the COP Presidency 
rotates among the five recognized UN regions - that is, 
Africa, Asia, Latin America, and the Caribbean, Central 
and Eastern Europe and Western Europe and Others – 
there is a tendency for the venue of the COP to also shift 
among these groups.

he Medium-Term Revenue and Expenditure

revenue forecasts and planned future expendi-

usually for a three-year period. 
It captures all FGN’s revenue sources and projected 
inflows, statutory outflows, distribution amongst the 
three tiers of government, anticipated expenses and 
other financial obligations whether domestic or foreign. 
The purpose is to enable the Government understand its 
three-year revenue generation and expenditure outlook 
to project its income and expenditure accordingly. MTREF 
is the economic framework on which the annual FGN 
Budget is prepared.

P

staff and contractors deliver on their job roles. 
This has brought an increase in Work from 
Home (WFH)schedules, Non-fixed hours workdays, 
Unmanned facilities, use of Office tools (Teams, Zoom, 
Dynamix) and so many other similar initiatives. Despite 
these seemingly workforce friendly adaptations, in 
the last few years there has been a marked increase 
in health-related fatalities in the Nigerian Oil and Gas 
Industry. In 2023, Port Harcourt Zone recorded seven 
(07) fatalities which consisted of four (04) non-work 
related and three (03) work related incidents. Similarly 
in 2022, five (05) non-work-related incidents were

would say that these statistics are relatively small but 
at percentage assessment, these show that more than 
50% of fatal incidents reported to the Commission are 
as a result of mental or physical health conditions. 
In recent years, there has been a growing awareness 
of the importance of mental health in the workplace, 
including the high-pressure environment of the Oil 
and Gas Industry in Nigeria. Companies operating in 
this industry have recognized the need to prioritize 
the mental well-being of their employees alongside 
maintaining a focus on physical safety and health. 
Most organisations now mandate routine Know Your 
Numbers (BP and weight) checks at resumption at any 
facility and in the office complex. There has also been a 
marked rise in annual physical health programmes such 
as Walk for Life and marathons.  
Despite these programmes, we still see a lot of men 
(most under 50) and some women succumb to the 
ill effects of poor mental and physical health. As the 
sole regulator for occupational health and safety in 
the Nigerian Upstream sector, there is a need to be at 
the forefront of securing the lives of each and every 
Nigerian working in the Industry. The Nigerian govern-
ment in recognition of the importance of the health 
and safety of the Nigerian workforce developed the 
National Policy on Occupational Safety and Health 
(2019) that aims to ensure the well-being of workers 
in all sectors. This policy emphasizes the prevention of 
occupational hazards, promotion of workers' health, 
and provision of support for workers with occupa-
tional health issues. This policy in conjunction with the 
Mineral Oil Safety Regulations (MOSR 1997) and other 
extant guidelines are the regulatory instruments which 
drive our mandate for ensuring personnel welfare in 
the Industry.
Organisations as a mitigative mechanism require 
enhanced enforcement of the various initiatives and 
best practices they may have adopted to prioritize 
employee well-being and mental health. Some of these 
practices include:
.

More companies especially our indigenous contractors 
need to organize awareness campaigns to educate 
employees about mental health issues, promote open 
discussions  and reduce stigma associated with these 
issues. These campaigns should include workshops, 
seminars, and training sessions on stress management, 
resilience, and mental health first aid. 
2.  Employee Assistance Programmes (EAP) 
EAPs provide confidential counselling and support 
services to employees and their families. These pro-
grammes offer resources for managing personal and

THE DOMESTIC GAS MARKET - STATE OF PLAY 
PRE-PIA 2021
With proven gas reserves of over 208 trillion cubic feet 
(tcf), and a potential upside of about 600tcf; Nigeria 
is ranked as the 9th largest gas reserves in the world 
and could be regarded as a gas province with some oil 
deposits. Despite this enviable position, Nigeria’s gas 
has over the years, been skewed in favour of export 
than the domestic market, making it more of a rent 
seeking commodity than a high value-added product. 
This grim situation was even direr up to the early 2000s 
when most major gas producers preferred the export of 
LNG.  In response to this undesirable trend, Government 
has identified accelerated development of the domestic 
gas sector as critical to the attainment of the national 
aspiration of growing the country’s GDP through a 
gas-based industrialization of the Naton’s economy for 
better living conditions of its growing population from 
our natural endowment. 
It must be noted however, that LNG exports provide 
high returns to government through tax receipts and 
dividends for equity stake. That, notwithstanding, there 
are broader strategic benefits to the economy, beyond 
economic rent, that can be achieved from deepened 
domestic utilization and in-country value addition to 
natural gas.  
Given the above background, the Domestic Gas 
Supply Obligation (DSO) was introduced in 2008 by the 
Government to balance the curve and grow supply to 
the domestic market. The DSO was an annual supply 
obligation to all gas producers, with the sum of all 
obligations equalling the planned national domestic 
demand requirement for gas. Although backed by 
a Regulation – National Gas Supply and Pricing 
Regulations, 2008, the framework failed to achieve its 
desired objective. Supply to the domestic gas market 
did not grow as expected or forecast. Notable gap in 
the DSO framework was that whereas gas supply 
obligations were allocated to producers, there was no 
explicit provision that warranted a producer to actually 
make the requisite effort (construction of a spur line) to 
deliver gas beyond its battery limit to a point accessible 
to an offtaker. A direct outcome of this regulatory gap 
is that most gas suppliers in the country continued to 
focus on LNG projects. 
DOMESTIC GAS MARKET AND THE PIA 2021
The Petroleum Industry Act (PIA) of 2021 represents a 
landmark legislation in the history of Nigeria's oil and 
gas industry. It embodies all the lessons learnt over 
the decades in the administration of Nigeria Oil and 
Gas Industry. Among others, the Act seeks to address 
impediments to the growth of the sector’s impact on 
Nigeria’s economy through streamlined and transpar-
ent institutional and regulatory provisions.  A critical 
component of the legislation is the Domestic Gas 
Delivery Obligation (DGDO), which is a strategic initia-
tive aimed at improving on the shortcomings of the 
Domestic Gas Supply Obligation (DSO). For instance, 
the legislation for the DGDO unlike the DSO, explicitly 
included provisions for delivery of gas to a location 
where it can be accessed by the offtaker.  The DGDO 
is designed to ensure the availability of gas for the 
strategic sectors which includes - Power, the Gas-Based 
Industries (GBI), and Commercial enterprises. The 
framework is designed to balance the pull between 
export and domestic markets with full cognizance 
that gas can be a catalyst for industrializing Nigeria’s 
economy and not just a rent seeking export commodity. 
This has become especially expedient as gas continues 
to grow in importance in industrialized nations, whereas 
their reserves continue to decline, resulting in the phe-
nomenon of rising gas prices in most key international 
markets and consequently creating a preferential pull 
for export LNG.
