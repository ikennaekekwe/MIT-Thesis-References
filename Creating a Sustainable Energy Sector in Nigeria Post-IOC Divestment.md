Abstract The Nigerian energy sector has witnessed a signiﬁcant transformation
due to the divestment of International Oil Companies (IOCs) from onshore and
shallow water oil assets. Driven by factors such as the global shift towards cleaner
energy, security concerns, and regulatory changes, IOCs have divested assets worth
approximately $21 billion as of 2023. Historically, IOCs like Shell, Chevron, and
Total Energies played a dominant role in Nigeria’s petroleum industry, contributing
signiﬁcantly to the nation’s GDP and export earnings. However, their exit from their
onshore and shallow water assets poses challenges and opportunities for the Nige-
rian economy and local oil companies, known as Domestic Oil Companies (DOCs).
DOCs have gained experience over the years and are now taking over marginal ﬁelds
divested by IOCs, boosting production in previously underutilized assets. Although
DOCs now control 30% of national oil production, DOCs are still faced with chal-
lenges like technical limitations, ﬁnancial constraints, and environmental risks. This
study examines the implications of these divestments and the beneﬁts of divestment
to the industry, government and academia. The divestment creates opportunities for
academia, fostering research collaborations and innovation within Nigeria’s universi-
ties, and the development of indigenous technologies and solutions. Divestment also
beneﬁts the industry and government by providing an opportunity for local ﬁrms to
drive the growth of the energy sector.

Introduction
The Nigerian energy sector, long dominated by International Oil Companies (IOCs),
is undergoing a transformative shift as these global entities continue to divest from
their onshore and shallow water assets. This divestment is driven by a conﬂu-
ence of factors, including the global energy transition towards cleaner sources, the
evolving regulatory environment in Nigeria, and prevailing environmental and secu-
rity concerns. As of 2023, the value of divested assets by IOCs had reached approx-
imately $21 billion, emphasizing the scale of this exit and its potential impact on
Nigeria’s energy landscape (Business Day).
Historically, International Oil Companies (IOCs) have been the backbone of
Nigeria’s oil and gas industry, overseeing the exploration, production, and expor-
tation of its vast energy resources. Major players like Shell, ExxonMobil, Chevron,
and TotalEnergies have not only driven operational activities but also made substan-
tial contributions to the nation’s economy, signiﬁcantly boosting Gross Domestic
Product (GDP) and generating vital government revenues. Improving the sector’s
contribution to GDP remains critical in a country heavily dependent on oil and
gas. In 2022, the sector accounted for approximately 6% of Nigeria’s GDP, yet it
dominated export earnings, contributing over 90% (Emediegwu & Okeke, 2017). As
shown in Fig. 16.1, this contrast highlights a core problem and emphasizes the need
for improved performance within the sector.
Marginal ﬁeld development has gained relevance in oil producing countries
because of the huge potential economic beneﬁts it offers. The Federal Government
of Nigeria commenced a Marginal Fields program in 2001 as part of her policy to
improve the nation’s strategic oil and gas reserves and promote indigenous partic-
ipation in the upstream sector. A growing trend of divestment by IOCs has posed
uncertainties over Nigeria’s energy future. Divestments have been driven by various

With IOCs ofﬂoading assets, shifting their focus away from onshore and shallow
water operations and the global shift towards cleaner alternatives, there are serious
concerns about whether Domestic Oil Companies (DOCs) possess the expertise,
infrastructure, and ﬁnancial capacity to ﬁll the gap and ensure the sector’s sustain-
ability. Nigeria has hugely beneﬁtted from the presence of many IOCs since the
inception of crude oil production at Oloibiri in 1956. DOCs springing up have devel-
oped competency over the years during the operations of the IOCs and are prepared
to take over the marginal divested assets and put them to production. DOCs have
evolved and prepared over the years to take over divested assets. Infact, the IOC
divestments is also driven by belief in the competency of local oil companies. DOCs
currently contribute 30% of national oil production and this will increase as more
land and shallow water assets are divested.
The implications of IOC divestment are multifaceted. On one hand, it presents
challenges such as potential declines in technical expertise and reduced access to
capital for DOCs that acquire these assets. On the other hand, it also offers oppor-
tunities for greater local content development, increased ownership of the sector by
Nigerian entities, and the potential for innovation driven by academia and research
institutions (Onwuka et al, 2021).
The ﬁrst signiﬁcant petroleum industry divestment in Nigeria occurred in 2010,
when Shell Petroleum Development Company (SPDC) sold some of its onshore oil
assets in the Niger Delta. This divestment marked the beginning of a broader trend of
International Oil Companies (IOCs) scaling back their onshore operations in Nigeria,
primarily due to challenges such as pipeline vandalism, oil theft, and community
unrest. Shell’s divestment included selling its stakes in various oil blocks to indige-
nous Nigerian companies, with Seplat Energy being one of the major beneﬁciaries
at the time (Iyua et al, 2016). This move paved the way for more indigenous ﬁrms to
take a larger role in the upstream oil and gas sector in Nigeria. Other IOCs, such as
Chevron, Total, and Eni, followed suit in subsequent years by divesting their onshore
assets (Akinlawon & Ildeare, 2018).
While the departure of IOCs presents an opportunity for greater local control
and content development, it also introduces several challenges that could destabi-
lize Nigeria’s energy sector if not properly managed. DOCs, which are expected to
take over the divested assets, face technical, ﬁnancial, and operational challenges.
Many DOCs do not have as much expertise as the IOCs, they have less capital,
and infrastructure to manage these assets effectively as the IOCs did. Furthermore,
environmental sustainability and security risks remain pressing concerns. Given that
Nigeria is still heavily reliant on oil revenue, failure to ensure the sustainability
of its energy sector post-IOC divestment could lead to severe economic outcomes,
including declining revenues and reduced energy security.
The evolving landscape also raises questions about Nigeria’s readiness to adapt
to global energy trends, particularly the shift toward cleaner energy sources. In this
context, academia, DOCs, and government policy must work collaboratively to foster
innovation, build local capacity, and ensure the long-term sustainability of the sector.

This study aims to investigate the implications of IOC divestment in Nigeria
energy sector and to explore the critical roles that academia, DOCs, and government
policy play in ensuring a sustainable and resilient energy industry in Nigeria. It
seeks to evaluate the capacity of DOCs to manage and enhance the assets acquired
from IOCs, the role of academic institutions in fostering innovation and building local
expertise, and the impact of government policies in creating an enabling environment
for the sector’s growth. The Triple Helix model, which emphasizes the interplay
between academia, industry, and government, serves as the theoretical framework
for this study. Through this lens, the paper examines how these three pillars can
collaborate to ensure the long-term sustainability of Nigeria’s energy sector.
This study signiﬁcantly lies in its ability to contribute to the discourse on how
Nigeria can create a sustainable energy sector in the face of IOC divestment. By
examining the potential for increased local participation, technological innovation,
and government interventions, this study provides a framework for how Nigeria can
mitigate the challenges posed by IOC divestment while capitalizing on the opportu-
nities for growth. Furthermore, it highlights the importance of academia in driving
research and development, ensuring that DOCs and policymakers are equipped with
the knowledge and tools needed to navigate the evolving energy landscape.

Nigeria’s oil and gas production is facing signiﬁcant challenges due to Western
Environmental, Social and Governance (ESG) strategies which are pressuring Inter-
national Oil Companies (IOCs) to reassess their global upstream and downstream
operations. This has led to major asset reshufﬂing and divestments. As one of
OPEC’s top oil producers, Nigeria has already experienced asset divestments valued
at $21 billion, raising concerns about the country’s energy future.
Divestment, also known as ‘divestiture,’ involves a company selling part of its
assets, typically to increase its overall value and improve operational efﬁciency
(Ogunbanjo et al, 2024). This strategy is commonly employed by companies to
shed non-core assets, allowing their management to concentrate more effectively on
the primary areas of their business.
As IOCs divest their assets and scale down their operations in Nigeria, the mantle
of managing these oil and gas assets increasingly falls on Domestic Oil Compa-
nies (DOCs). The success of this transfer of responsibility depends largely on the
ability of DOCs to manage, maintain, and improve the assets acquired efﬁciently.
The potential for DOCs to play a pivotal role in sustaining Nigeria’s energy sector is
signiﬁcant; however, their capacity is challenged by various factors including ﬁnan-
cial constraints, technological limitations, environmental compliance, and human
resource capabilities (Adewuyi et al., 2020).
One of the major challenges facing DOCs in managing the acquired assets from
IOCs is the capital-intensive nature of oil and gas operations. IOCs are known for
their deep pockets and their ability to ﬁnance large-scale projects with relative ease.
Nalule et al., emphasize that the required CAPEX to maintain oilﬁelds, pipelines,
and reﬁneries can be overwhelming for DOCs without access to the same level
of international ﬁnancing enjoyed by the IOCs. Another critical area affecting the
capacity of DOCs to manage acquired assets is their technological preparedness.
IOCs have historically had access to cutting-edge technologies, enabling them to
maximize extraction efﬁciency, reduce environmental impact, and ensure operational
safety. Ekeinde et al (2022) describe the technological gap between IOCs and DOCs
as seen in areas such as seismic data acquisition, digital well management, and
enhanced oil recovery (EOR) techniques.
Etokudoh et al., (2017) emphasize that human capital development poses a consid-
erable challenge for DOCs. While IOCs have historically been leaders in cultivating
local talent through training, research collaborations, and global exchange programs,
DOCs have limited access to similar opportunities. Many DOCs may be ill-equipped
to handle the environmental management systems required to mitigate oil spills,
gas ﬂaring, and other ecological concerns that plague the Niger Delta region. While
the Petroleum Industry Act (PIA) was designed to provide a more transparent and
business-friendly environment, DOCs often ﬁnd it difﬁcult to navigate the bureau-
cratic processes involved in obtaining licenses, complying with environmental laws,
and managing the relationship with local communities.
While challenges remain, there are opportunities for DOCs to grow and strengthen
their capacity. Partnerships with international service companies, government incen-
tives for local content development, and collaborations with academia can create
a more enabling environment for DOCs to thrive. Furthermore, innovation in the

Impact of the 2009 Divestment on Oil Production
The ﬁrst notable divestment in Nigeria’s oil and gas industry was in 2009 when Shell
sold four onshore oil blocks. This event triggered a wave of divestments that changed
the trajectory of the Nigerian oil industry. Seplat Energy is one of the companies that
purchased the assets from Shell. Seplat Energy acquired a 45% working interest in,
and was appointed operator of, a portfolio of three onshore producing oil and gas
leases: OMLs 4, 38 and 41. Initially Seplat formed a JV partnership with NNPC,
until NNPC transferred its 55% interest to NPDC. (seplat.com) The ﬁelds had been
producing around 18,000 b/d until they were shut in following a militant attack on
the main delivery pipeline in late 2008 (woodmac.com). Today, Seplat operates the
blocks on behalf of the Seplat/NPDC joint venture. By 2019, about 10 years after
Seplat acquired the three onshore producing oil and gas leases, Seplat had grown
to her portfolio to 8 oil blocks, 46,498 boepd WIP and 509 MMboe 2P reserves.

Impact of Divestment on the Government
Divestment in Nigeria’s petroleum sector has generated several key beneﬁts for
the government. A major advantage is increased local participation, as indigenous
companies acquire assets previously held by IOCs, fostering local industry growth
and keeping more revenue within the country. This shift also generates revenue from
asset sales as new operators pay fees, royalties, and taxes to the government.
The government beneﬁts from job creation as local companies tend to hire
more Nigerian workers, boosting employment and skill development. Additionally,
divestment provides the government with greater control over resources, enabling
more inﬂuence over policies that align with national interests, such as local content
development and sustainability.

Impact of Divestment on the Academia
The divestment of Nigeria’s petroleum sector, particularly by International Oil
Companies (IOCs), has signiﬁcantly impacted academia, creating new opportuni-
ties and challenges. As indigenous companies take over oil and gas assets, there is an
increased demand for local expertise, prompting Nigerian universities to adapt and
strengthen their collaboration with the industry. One key impact is the enhanced
partnership between academia and local ﬁrms. With the exit of IOCs, Nigerian
companies are turning to academic institutions for research and solutions to tech-
nical challenges. This has resulted in more research collaborations, internships, and
consultancy opportunities for academics, linking universities more closely with the
industry.
Additionally, curriculum development has become a priority. To meet the demands
of a more locally-driven petroleum sector, Nigerian universities are revising their
programs in engineering, geology, and environmental science. The focus is now
on indigenous technology, local content policies, and sustainable energy practices,
ensuring graduates are better equipped for the evolving industry.
Moreover, divestment has created new research opportunities. Local ﬁrms are
investing in research, offering academics a chance to contribute to the industry’s
growth. This has led to increased funding and support for innovation in the energy
sector. DOCs can now use locally formulated chemicals in their ﬁelds instead of
importing everything as was previously done by IOCs. This will boost oil and gas
research in Nigeria as most DOCs will fund speciﬁc university research tailored to
improving the DOCs production. The Nigerian Higher education institutions will
greatly beneﬁt from this as funding will provide equipment for research which was

Conclusion
The divestments of oil ﬁelds by International Oil Companies (IOCs) in Nigeria
have led to signiﬁcant changes in the Nigerian oil and gas industry, affecting local
production, investment, governance and academia. One of the most notable effects
of the divestments is the rise of indigenous Nigerian oil and gas companies. Many
of these companies acquired the divested assets, marking a shift in the landscape of
Nigeria’s upstream sector. The transactions have enabled these local ﬁrms to scale up
production and capacity, boosting local content development. This has also enhanced
the partnership between the industry and academia. These companies can support
research in the universities and apply output of these research on their ﬁelds. Divest-
ment has improved the overall oil production in Nigeria and opened up opportunities
for developing the gas ﬁelds. The government, industry and academia have beneﬁted
from the divestment as there is increased local participation in the industry, more
research funding for the academia and increase in revenue for the country due to
increased production.

Toyin Olabisi Odutola is an Associate Professor at the
Department of Petroleum and Gas Engineering and the Assis-
tant Director at Emerald Energy Institute, University of Port
Harcourt (UNIPORT). She holds a bachelor’s degree in
chemical engineering from Ladoke Akintola University of
Technology, a master’s degree in petroleum engineering from
the African University of Science and Technology (AUST)
and a doctorate in petroleum engineering from UNIPORT.
She is trained in Flow Assurance at the Center for Hydrate
Research at the Colorado School of Mines, USA. She designed
and fabricated Nigeria’s ﬁrst hydrate ﬂow loop, and her work
was featured by the British Broadcasting Corporation (BBC)
in 2022. She has three patents, a published monograph and
several peer-reviewed papers in reputable journals. She was a
visiting scholar at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology
(MIT), USA, in 2020. She is a fellow of the MIT Empowering
the Teachers Programme (MIT-ETT), USA and a fellow of the
Nigeria Science Leadership Programme (N-SLP). Dr Odutola
is a registered engineer with the Council for the Regulation of
Engineering in Nigeria (COREN), she is a corporate member
of the Society of Petroleum Engineers, Nigerian Society of
Engineers and Nigerian Society of Chemical Engineers. Dr
Odutola has received several awards, the most recent of which
is the 2024 SPE Africa Regional Distinguished Achievement
Award for Petroleum Engineering Faculty Award.
