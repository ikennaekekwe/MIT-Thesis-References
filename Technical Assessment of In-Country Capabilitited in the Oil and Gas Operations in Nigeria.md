Abstract
Building local capacity among indigenous oil and gas players is a non-negotiable mandate for the Nigerian
Oil and Gas Industry. A technical evaluation was conducted to assess the capabilities of 33 indigenous
companies rendering services ranging from Tubing Inspection Handling and Management Services,
Slickline Operations and, Surface Well Testing Operations within the Warri and Port-Harcourt areas of
the Niger Delta. The table top and facility inspection evaluations showed that, for the Tubing Inspection
Services, 44% out of the 18 companies sampled scored the mandatory 60% minimum required to qualify
technically. Surface well testing services showed 63% of the 8 companies scoring above 70% while
Slickline services revealed 88% pass for the 8 companies sampled. Criteria used in the technical
assessment of these companies include; equipment and materials availability, service delivery time, job
experience, HSE standards, personnel, staff training and project management. This paper attempts to
evaluate the technical capabilities of indigenous companies as well as highlight some of the challenges
facing the local content initiative.
Introduction
A major stimulation of growth in local capacity within the Nigerian oil and gas sector is the enactment
of the Nigerian Oil and Gas Content Development Act in 2010. This Act gives priority to indigenous
Nigerian Companies in the awarding of oil and gas related contracts and projects; the recruitment and
training of Nigerians; and the provision of succession plan for positions not occupied by Nigerians in
International Oil Companies (IOCs) operating within Nigeria for the main purpose of building in-country
capacity. This in the long run has resulted in the establishment of small and medium scale indigenous
companies, mostly by experienced professionals who have once worked in these IOCs for a considerably
long period of time.
It is pertinent to mention that the corner stone for effective local content development is the
development of technical capacities in terms of human resources and related infrastructure. Over the years
there have been numerous commentaries and emphases on the need for greater participation of local
players. However, very little has been done in the area of assessing how technically equipped they are or
how much progress has been made. It is possible for local companies to secure contracts through the
backing of multinationals or foreign partners without necessarily having the required technical expertise.

In that case, achieving the goal of the Nigerian local content initiative may be far-fetched. There is the
need for periodic evaluation with a view to finding ways to ensure local companies improve their technical
standing in the industry where gaps abound. This is in line with the objective of this paper.
Some major challenges affecting small and medium scale indigenous companies include huge capital,
high operational risk and issues with deployment of new technology (Udoh, et al. 2015). There also is the
issue of non-compliance with the Nigerian Local Content directives and requirements by large operating
companies (Aniche, 2014). These challenges adversely affect competitiveness, the acquisition of skills
and the required technical expertise to deliver on the job, (Omenikolo & Amadi, 2010). Also on the part
of the government, challenges range from unstable political climate, lack of investment in infrastructure,
poor attention to research and development and poor financing of small and medium scale companies thus
impeding the 70% local content drive target set in 2010 (Omenikolo & Amadi, 2010).
The Nigerian Petroleum Development Company (NPDC) Ltd is a fully owned subsidiary of the
Nigerian National Petroleum Corporation (NNPC), a key driver of the Nigerian local content initiative. Its
major upstream activities include oil & gas exploration, exploitation and production activities both
onshore and offshore in the hydrocarbon-rich regions Niger Delta and Equatorial Guinea. This paper
presents results of technical evaluations of companies within the Warri and Port-Harcourt areas of the
Niger Delta showing their technical capabilities to execute three (3) of the essential well services awarded
by NPDC to competent service companies. These include Tubing Inspection Handling and Management
Services, Slickline Operations and, Surface Well Testing Operations. A table top evaluation of their
submissions was first made followed by field inspections based on approved technical criteria.
Tubing inspection, handling and management services are usually deployed during well completion
operations. The technical capabilities required to effectively render tubing inspection service include: a)
the provision of full length magnetic particle inspection of pipe body (MPI), b) ultrasonic wall thickness
reading, c) API full length drift test (FDT), d) steel grade comparison/verification (SGC), e) end
area/thread inspection (EAI), f) full body finishing visual inspection (FVI), g) pipe finishing/thread
protection. The tubing handling and management services aspect however, requires technical expertise in
a) supervision of the off-loading and stacking of all joints of the chromium tubing at the appropriate
positions at the rig site and on the rig b) visual full body length and thread inspections of all joints of
tubing on the site to ensure joints are acceptable for running in hole, c) measuring tallying and racking
required number of joints based on completion tally, d) assign numbers and indicate actual tubing length
on the body of every joint and submit tubing tally to NPDC Company man e) preparation of reports.
Surface well testing services is also required during well completions. The technical expertise required
include: a) the provision and manning of surface well testing equipment during well completions, b)
carrying out tests on black oil, condensate and gas wells, c) collection of separator samples of oil and gas
for PVT analysis and water samples for laboratory analysis, d) preparation of well tests and analysis
reports. Slickline services are also deployed during well completion/workover operations, well mainte-
nance, repairs and BHP surveys. The needed expertise include a) running, setting and pulling of
down-hole (subsurface) production equipment, b) fishing and retrieval of tolls and/or extraneous objects
dropped in hole, c) swabbing and inducing well to flow through successive bailing out of fluid thereby
reducing the hydrostatic pressure on the formation, d) tubing integrity tests, c) opening/closing and
installation of flow control devices d) zonal isolation.

Results and Discussion
Assessment of capabilities in tubing inspection, handling and management services
The results of the technical evaluation conducted for eighteen (18) indigenous companies who tendered
for the tubing inspection, handling and management services showed that only 44% scored the mandatory
60% benchmark required to qualify technically. The 56% failure recorded was due to the lack of adequate
personnel, tools, and equipment observed during the facility inspection of these indigenous companies.
Although the table top evaluations for 9 out of these 10 companies showed high pass marks of 25-35%
relative to a maximum of 50%, the facility inspection evaluations scores revealed deficiencies and
technical gaps within the companies.

Two of these companies, C5 and C7 had peculiar situations. The former passed the table top evaluation
with a score of 35% but failed the facility inspection evaluation (14% score) due to lack of adequate tools
and equipment. However, this company had adequate personnel who showed a reasonable level of
competence. The latter failed the table top evaluation by scoring only 11%. However, facility inspection
showed it had a memorandum of understanding (MoU) with another company for services it does not
provide. Although it had 37% for facility inspection, its low table top score disqualified the company
technically. It is possible that the company poorly packaged its tender documents or that there was an error
in its submission. The individual challenges demonstrated by the disqualified companies could be ascribed
to funding challenges which ultimately affects their operational capabilities (Balouga, 2012; Vaaland et
al 2012).
Assessment of capabilities in surface well testing services
Out of the seven (7) companies that submitted technical bids for surface well testing services, two (2)
companies, D3 and D6, had scores below the bench mark score of 60% Although they obtained good table
top evaluation scores of 31 and 36%, the field inspection evaluations were below 16%, this disqualifying
them technically. These companies lacked adequate personnel, equipment and tools needed to deliver the
required services. Figure 2 presents the technical evaluation performances of each company against the
bench mark score.
Results also showed 63% of the companies scoring between 71 to 78%. These companies constitute

Assessment of capabilities in slickline services
Results of the technical evaluation conducted for eight (8) indigenous companies who submitted bids for
the slickline services showed seven (7) companies scoring above the mandatory 60% benchmark required
to qualify technically. The only company who failed technically (E5) was not located at the given address
contained in the technical bid. This invariably is a ‘portfolio’ company without operational base and
structure. Figure 4 presents the technical evaluation performances of each company against the bench
mark score.
Limitation of study
This study was somewhat limited by the number of tenders submitted within the given time frame. Also
these three (3) services only focused on land and swamp based operations.

analysis, it is clear that the international service brands and local entities that are affiliated with them have
the comparative advantage. The aim of the local content drive is to see more local firms beginning to stand

This study shows that local players in the oil and gas industry have made notable progress in terms of
human resources and development of infrastructure. It is also evident that quite a number of local players
in the oil and gas industry are grappling with lack of funds which drastically affects their operational
capabilities. However, it is fascinating to observe that for services with greater technical complexities and
huge capital requirement, local operators involved seem to do better technically than it is the case with
less complex and less capital intensive services.
