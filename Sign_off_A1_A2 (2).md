## A1 — Statutory citation correction: "Section 29 of the Petroleum Act" -> First Schedule paragraphs 14 and 16

**Verified basis (Petroleum Act 1969, First Schedule).** Paragraph 14: "Without the prior consent of the Minister, the holder of an oil prospecting licence or an oil mining lease shall not assign his licence or lease, or any right, power or interest therein." Paragraph 16: "The Minister shall not give his consent to an assignment unless he is satisfied that... (b) there is likely to be available to the proposed assignee... sufficient technical knowledge and experience and sufficient financial resources." Revocation sits at paragraphs 26 to 30. "Section 29" is a miscitation on both counts: assignment consent is paragraph 14, the criteria are paragraph 16, and 29 is in the revocation cluster.

## A2 — Reframe: "the mandate did not exist" / "the Act was silent" -> the mandate existed since 1969 but lacked operational specificity

**The reframe, in one line.** A general technical-competence requirement has existed continuously since 1969 (paragraph 16(b): "sufficient technical knowledge and experience"); what the framework lacked, until the NUPRC's 2024 pillar, was *operational specificity* for asset-specific operating capability, crisis-response readiness, and organizational absorptive capacity.

**Why this strengthens rather than weakens the thesis.** The current text asserts a total absence ("the mandate did not exist"), which a reviewer can demolish in one sentence by quoting paragraph 16(b). The reframe states the more accurate and more defensible claim and locates the gap precisely.

**Verdict preservation (the critical point).** The verdict on regulatory inadequacy stays "an enabling condition insufficient on its own." The discriminating logic is untouched: the same pre-2024 framework, whether read as silent or as under-specified, governed every operator, yet outcomes diverged, so the framework cannot be the differentiating cause. Chapter 2 line 83 (the differentiation paragraph) is preserved verbatim and is the anchor that holds the verdict in place.

**The one caveat to watch in execution.** The reframe moves the regulatory story from "no rule" to "rule under-specified and unenforced," which sits closer to a regulatory-failure narrative. The wording must not let regulatory inadequacy drift toward "primary driver." Every reframed passage must keep the "enabling, insufficient" framing and the same-framework/different-outcomes argument adjacent.

### Locus A2-1 — Chapter 2, design-failure paragraph (line 73) [PRIMARY]
CURRENT: "Design failure holds that the regulatory framework was structurally incomplete. It did not include operational capability assessment as a criterion for divestment approval. The mandate did not exist. The regulator assessed what the rules required, specifically financial capacity, legal compliance, and equity structure, and did not assess what the rules did not cover: embedded operational knowledge, crisis response capability, and organizational readiness."
PROPOSED: "Design failure holds that the regulatory framework was structurally incomplete on the dimension that mattered. A general technical-competence criterion existed: paragraph 16(b) of the First Schedule to the Petroleum Act required that the assignee have available sufficient technical knowledge and experience. What the framework lacked was operational specificity: it set no test of the asset-specific operating capability, crisis-response readiness, or organizational absorptive capacity that the cases show to be decisive. The regulator assessed what the rules made specific, financial capacity, legal compliance, and equity structure, and did not assess what they left general: embedded operational knowledge, crisis-response capability, and organizational readiness."
RATIONALE: Replaces the false total-absence claim with the accurate under-specification claim. Preserves the design-failure verdict and the institutional-economics grounding that follows (North, 1990).
