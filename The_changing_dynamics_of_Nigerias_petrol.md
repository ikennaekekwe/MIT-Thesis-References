SUMMARY
This debate examines the changing dynamics of the Nigerian economy and politics. 
It discusses the consequences of the downward trend of petroleum revenues and 
new energy technologies in the transport sector and the impact on Nigeria’s polit-
ical and institutional structures in the ‘post-oil economy’. It argues that although 
the reductions in oil revenues due to internal and global factors have led to radical 
debates on political restructuring, the post-oil transitions are more likely to be cha-
otic than orderly. There seems to be only a small hope that the reactionary ruling 
class will willingly withdraw from influencing the national economy while the oil 
economy continues, even in a reduced capacity.

Political economy; ‘petrostate’; decarbonisation; COP28; post-oil economy; state 
viability; political restructuring

In the second phase (1970–2015, referred to by some as the country’s ‘petrostate’ 
period1), with the oil boom in the 1970s, petro-politics became the backbone of the 
Nigerian federal state. Hydrocarbon extraction provided a major part of government 
revenue (Pérouse de Montclos 2014, 403). The military redesigned Nigeria’s federal 
structure, moving from four regions to one based on 36 states. All levels of govern-
ment depended exclusively on revenues from the sale of crude oil. However, more 
than 95% of the petroleum resources were produced in the nine states of the Niger 
Delta in southern Nigeria: Abia, Akwa Ibom, Bayelsa, Cross River, Delta, Edo, Imo, 
Ondo and Rivers. Within this phase, mining rents and royalties became more buoy-
ant than any other source of income in the country. These developments signified 
a change of focus in the accumulation process from peasant-based, regionally con-
trolled agriculture to federally regulated oil rents and royalties. The geographically 
concentrated oil wealth transformed Nigeria into a redistributive state, with reve-
nue-sharing mechanisms requiring wealthier oil states to subsidise poorer states. 
This encouraged multiple structures: Nigeria currently has 36 states, 774 local gov-
ernment areas and its Federal Capital Territory. The creation of states and local gov-
ernment areas became an instrument for extracting resources and rents from the 
Nigerian state by the elite and ethnic formations. In this period agitation for control 
or benefit from petroleum oil wealth distribution became central to politics.
The third phase ushered in a rise in the collection of taxes on non-oil earnings as 
the result of a decline in oil prices and revenue (Burns and Owen 2019, 3). A question 
now being explored is to what extent Nigeria has entered a post-oil economy – and 
what are some of the consequences if it has? Nigerians were accustomed to paying 
fewer taxes in the former oil economy because the state was able to rely on royal-
ties to meet its expenditure. In the later, post-oil, period, the government needs to 
coerce and extract resources from its citizens (Guyer 1992, in Pérouse de Montclos 
2014, 404). According to a BusinessDay editorial (2024), the divestment of multina-
tional oil companies ‘is an ominous signal that the golden era of Nigeria’s oil wealth 
has ended. … In a rapidly decarbonizing world, Nigeria’s oil wealth is becoming a 
relic of the past.’
The issue of the Nigerian economy in the post-oil period started receiving atten-
tion after the 2015 oil price crash, when it fell ‘from [US]$112 in 2014 to [US]$53 in 
2015 … [reaching] around [US]$38 in 2016’ (Idowu and Bamidele 2018, 63), and 
more recently with the global crisis influenced by the Covid-19 pandemic, declining 
investment in the oil sector, continued divestments by international oil companies 
and their refocusing on cleaner energy (Quarshie and George-Ikoli 2022). Recognising 
the dangers of Nigeria’s continued dependency on oil has led to several initiatives 
to secure changes in government policy and structures to address the problem. 
Politicians, scholars and economic analysts have advocated for a ‘restructuring’ of

Nigeria’s politics to match the changes in the economy and ensure the country’s 
survival in a post-oil economy. Nigeria’s current political structure was institutionally 
designed in the context of the consumption of oil rents and has become outdated as 
a result of the changes in the economic base. A new structure is therefore needed to 
manage the post-oil economy effectively.
Three models have been advocated by scholars:
• soft restructuring: tinkering by amending the 1999 Constitution and devolving 
more power and finance to the existing lower levels of government (state and 
local);
• hybrid restructuring: negotiating a new constitution with a drastic reduction in 
the number of states – a return to an efficient, cost-effective regional structure 
with regional autonomy within the federation (six or eight regions, at most, 
from the amalgamation of the current 36 states); and
• hard restructuring: confederation or allowing outright secession for parts of 
the country that demand it (Soludo 2018; Egwim 2020). According to Obaseki, 
governor of Edo state from 2016 to 2024, ‘the problem with Nigeria today is 
structural. The structure we have is expired; it’s outdated. We need a new 
structure to run the economy of the state. If it doesn’t happen, we are not 
going anywhere’ (Shaibu 2024).
Examining the inter-relatedness of political and economic processes has led to 
theorising about what economic and political restructuring would be suitable for the 
current taxed earnings economy. Chukwuma Soludo (2018, 2019), who is governor 
of the state of Anambra and an economist, advocated changing Nigerian political 
institutions, arguing that this would change the persisting economic structure: ‘Our 
thesis is that if you want to change a persisting economic structure, [then] change 
the underlying institutions…. In the end, politics will always trump economicsʼ 
(Soludo 2019).
In contrast, this contribution to the debate distinguishes between the acts of cre-
ation by individuals and, on the other hand, social formations that emerge from the 
force of their existing economic situation, irrespective of the wishes of individual 
actors. Karl Marx suggested that in order to bring about a change in the economic 
structure, changes in the superstructure of the political system, ideology and cul-
ture would be necessitated, along with the possibility of revolution. We agree with 
Wang (1977, 8–9) that a change in the economic structure would either rapidly (by 
revolution) or slowly (through reforms) change the superstructure of politics and 
society. This is to say that when the old economic structure disintegrates, the super-
structure built upon this foundation must also be transformed. According to Wang 
(1977, 5), the rate of this disintegration may vary because ‘the reactionary classes 
do not willingly bow out of the historical stage with the disappearance of the old 
economic substructure.’ We therefore re-examined the restructuring debate pre-
sented by Soludo (2018, 2019), with his emphasis on the importance of changing the 
underlying institutions. We argue that the change in the economic structure already 
in motion in the Nigerian post-oil-economy would change the character of Nigeria’s 
political institutions and structures, whether state actors like it or not. However, the 
post-oil transition in Nigeria is more likely to be chaotic than orderly because the

reactionary class, and the regions and states which benefited from the oil economy, 
will ‘not willingly bow out of the historical stage’ (ibid.). The Nigerian state is pres-
ently constituted to serve the ruling class, which is linked to the oil economy and 
their capital accumulation interests. This must be considered in the debate on the 
country’s political development.
Nigeria has been described as ‘a nation stuck in the twilight of an oil age that has 
passed it by’ (BusinessDay 2024). Obaseki’s view, reported in Punch newspaper, was 
that ‘The Federal Government was “stuck in the past” and needed to restructure the 
country for progress’ (Shaibu 2024). According to Awogbenle (2024), ‘the climate deal 
[from COP28] on fossil transition effectively marks the beginning of the end for fossil 
fuels.’ Among the case studies of Azerbaijan, Canada, Colombia, Indonesia, Nigeria 
and Qatar, Nigeria is the most fragile and vulnerable to decarbonisation (Oberthür 
et al. 2022, 25). The fortune of Nigeria’s oil economy was closely tied to oil prices, 
making it vulnerable to oil market fluctuations. The phase-out of fossil fuels must 
usher in a new economic approach and reconstituted political institutions. As Soludo 
emphasised in his 2018 lecture, ‘Restructuring is not just a political agitation: it is the 
foundational plan for Nigeria’s future prosperity without oil’ (Soludo 2018).
Resistance to fiscal and institutional restructuring in Nigeria is anchored mainly 
on reliance on the old economic structure and the redistribution of petroleum 
resources. According to Soludo (2018), the current structure of Nigerian federalism 
represents Nigerians’ old economic ‘experience with oil and the institutional arrange-
ments to share and consume it’. In Nigeria’s petroleum economy, the Constitution’s 
legal and governance architecture was designed to share and consume the oil rents 
(Soludo 2018, 2019). Yet the contradictions of the oil-based economy, the decline 
in oil prices and revenue and the downward trend from decarbonisation and new 
energy technology in the transport sector require a political restructuring for the 
country to survive either through a revolution or reform of the political institutions. 
Such political restructuring will necessitate a move away from the underlying politics 
that characterised the petroleum economy.
Nigeria’s petroleum and politics
The increased inflow of mineral oil revenues from the early 1970s contributed to the 
emergence of a highly centralised federal system. Other factors contributing to this 
system included the increase in the number of states, military rule and the experi-
ence of the country’s civil war (1967–70). Through the prosecution of civil war, the 
dominant oil economy and military-guided constitutions, Nigeria’s political power 
consistently shifted towards the federal government. The shrinking size of the fed-
erating states compared to the former regions enhanced both the central govern-
ment’s power and the political appetite of the politicians who wished to control it. 
While federal government revenue from petroleum expanded, revenue attributed to 
the state fell drastically due to the increase in states from three regions in the 1960s 
to 36 states and the reduced emphasis on cash crops. The miniature states were on 
the verge of bankruptcy whenever they experienced a drop in federal allocations.

Out of the 36 federation states, Lagos is the only state that has been consecutively 
viable over the seven years studied. Many states remained unviable without monthly 
disbursements from the Federation Account Allocation Committee. Almost all the 
states and local councils struggled to pay salaries or maintain basic operational 
expenses, and there were defaults in the payment of wages and pensions and in 
the delivery of essential services. All layers of government (national, state and local) 
heavily depended on petroleum contributions.
Non-viability of states resulted from the decline in oil-generated revenue, a con-
sequence of the fall in oil prices in the global oil market. Almost all states except 
Lagos struggled to pay workers’ salaries as their internally generated revenue was far 
less than their running costs. Tax revenues from the oil and gas sector provided the 
largest share of the government budget; federal, state and local government spend-
ing depended on production levels and international commodity markets (Oberthür 
et al. 2022, 25).
The post-oil economy
According to Burns and Owen (2019, 6), Nigeria has already entered a post-oil 
 economy: ‘since 1971, the Federal Government of Nigeria has earned more from oil 
revenues than non-oil right up until 2016, marking a 45-year era of oil dominance.’ 
Soludo, in September 2024, supported this claim, stating that the Nigerian govern-
ment had been relying on remittances from the Nigeria Customs Service and taxes to 
pay monthly allocations to state governments:
Nigeria’s oil money has finished. Are you people hearing me? Since February, March, 
April, May, and this June that just ended, NNPC [the Nigerian National Petroleum 
Corporation] has not paid any money into the Federation Account from oil revenue. 
What we (federal, state and local governments) have been sharing (as monthly allo-
cations) are revenues generated by customs service, taxes etc. It is good for everyone 
to know this today. (Ugwu 2024)

Nigeria’s public finances earned more from non-oil sources than from oil revenues in 
2021, 2022 and 2023 (as shown in Figure 1). As Burns and Owen note (2019, 3), ‘The 
transformation to a post-oil future is already in the past.’
The transformation from petroleum to a tax-based economy came from the 
rise in non-oil tax collection and the decline in oil prices and revenue. The latter 
emerged from different threats to the petroleum economy, including massive crude 
oil theft, pipeline vandalism, declining investment in the oil sector and divestments 
by multinational oil companies (Anyaogu 2022; Investors King 2023; Salau 2023; 
Widdershoven 2023).
Huge subsidy payments on premium motor spirits (PMS), that is petrol, resulting 
from the difference between higher prices of imported fuel products and regulated 
pump prices, further depleted the government’s scarce revenues (Dan-Awoh 2023; 
Eboh 2023; Esiedesa 2023).
The commercialisation of the NNPC, with its transition from a public corporation 
to a limited liability company – NNPC/L – following the Petroleum Industry Act 2021, 
has raised the fear that the new status of the NNPC/L means that it may no longer 
pay oil rent to the Federation Accounts Allocation Committee, which has usually dis-
tributed the rent monthly to the three tiers of government. The component unit of 
the federation – federal, state or local government – may earn yearly dividends when 
the company reports profits (Kingsley 2022; Izuaka 2023). Oladehinde (2024) reports 
that ‘NNPC/L dividends to [the federal government] dropped by 89%, two years after 
[the Petroleum Industry Act of 2021] kicked in.’

VAT as part of the non-oil tax revenue
The new revenue base has changed to earnings-based taxpayer income contributions 
from the Nigeria Customs Service and the Federal Inland Revenue Service: that is, 
income derived mainly from local taxation. Taiwo Oyedele, fiscal policy partner and 
Africa tax leader at PricewaterhouseCoopers, commenting on the controversy over 
the collection of value-added tax (VAT) in Nigeria, explained in 2021 that ‘seven taxes 
give Nigeria about 98% of its revenue, namely Personal Income Tax, Property Tax, 
Stamp Duties, Petroleum Profits Tax, VAT, Companies Income Tax and Customs and 
Excise Tariff’ (Ajaja 2021). The states collect personal income tax, property tax and 
stamp duties. The other four are collected centrally but are still shared with states.
The most controversial tax, reflecting the old fiscal politics of the petroleum 
economy, is VAT. In 2020, N1.53 trillion was realised from VAT, higher than the N1.41 
trillion from Company Income Tax and slightly less than the N1.6 trillion collected 
by the Nigeria Customs Service (Punch 2021). VAT was introduced in 1993, replac-
ing sales tax. The 36 states of the federation and the Federal Capital Territory pay 
VAT to the federal government, thus pooling all the resources to share among the 
federal, state and local councils. In 2023 the federal government unveiled ambitious 
plans to bolster VAT, a 7.5% consumption tax, a sustained revenue source that has 
contributed significantly to government revenue at all levels. Fiscal policy projects a 
substantial increase in VAT collection, from an average of N35 trillion in 2024 to N40 
trillion in 2025 and N45 trillion in 2026 (Investors King 2023).
Like oil rents in the petroleum economy, VAT has become the centre of fiscal 
conflict between the federal government and the states, generating considerable 
comment and debate in the national press. Godwin Owoh, a professor of applied 
economics, argues that ‘VAT is a consumption tax, and you cannot consume in proxy. 
I cannot eat bread in Enugu on behalf of somebody in Lagos. VAT should be loca-
tion-specific, which is the intention of the Constitution’ (Tolu-Kolawole 2021). The 
federal focus on redistributing revenue from VAT has generated a battle for the col-
lection of VAT between the states and the federal government. Unlike the 1960s 
agro-economy and similar to the petroleum economy, states’ opportunities for 
the collection of VAT are not evenly distributed. Former Minister of Finance Kemi 
Adeosun ‘revealed that 55% of the [national] VAT revenue in 2017 was collected in 
Lagos State’ (Tolu-Kolawole 2021). An editorial in Punch noted that ‘Lagos and Rivers 
contribute over 70% of VAT, others contribute very little’ (Punch 2021).
In a legal struggle, a Federal High Court declared that the states did have the 
powers to collect VAT (PLAC 2021; Tolu-Kolawole 2021). However, the Federal 
Inland Revenue Service, a VAT-collecting agency of the federal government, sought 
to amend the Constitution to include VAT collection in the exclusive legislative list. 
Like the oil economy, the discourse has shifted from VAT being part of an autono-
mous state’s income generation to the usual inter-ethnic conflict between the north 
and south of the country (Ajaja 2021). Accounting for the injustice of redistribution 
of VAT, Governor Nyesom Wike notes that Rivers state (south) ‘generated N15bn 
VAT revenue in June 2021 but received only N4.7bn … Kano state [north] gener-
ated N2.8bn in the same month and received the same N2.8bn while … Lagos state 
[south] generated N46.4bn but received N9.3bn’ (Ajaja 2021). A developing debate

about VAT derivation mirrors older arguments around oil revenue derivation from

and control of resources in the post-oil economy; and, as with oil revenues, it seems

Subsidies and the tax economy
Nigeria has been struggling to boost revenues to fund its budget after reductions in 
oil income. In February 2020, the government raised the VAT rate from 5% to 7.5% 
in order to boost tax revenue. With the removal of subsidies, the petrol price (PMS) 
rose from N145/litre to N620/litre in October 2023 and reached N1,030 in November 
2024. There has been deregulation across all the elements in the petroleum sec-
tor upstream, downstream and midstream, as well as the commercialisation of the 
NNPC to free up funds for the various tiers of government. Reducing government 
social and welfare expenditure by removing PMS subsidies is expected to attract 
more investments, as corporate organisations and multinational oil firms, reluctant 
to invest in refineries, will be encouraged, according to Timipre Sylva, Minister of 
State for Petroleum (Iroanusi 2020). However, the policies have increased petroleum 
product prices, affecting other commodities and services and making life harder for 
many Nigerians. The electricity tariff moved from about N30.23 per kWh to about 
N62.33 per kWh in the first week of September 2020, and reached N209.5/kWh in 
July 2024 for tariff Band A, the consumers that pay the highest rate in return for a 
minimum of 20 hours of electricity supply per day. It is worth noting that, despite 
the rise of non-oil income and the removal of subsidies, the amount generated from 
these sources remains too low to cover the government’s total expenditure without 
the country’s political structures being aligned with the dictates of the economy.
Post-oil economy and political restructuring
Burns and Owen (2019, 3, original emphasis) argue that ‘while Nigeria’s political 
economy seems to have reached a tipping-point away from oil, core institutions 
and policies continue to lag behind as they remain structured around assumptions 
that oil is central’ to the economy. Fiscal policies, spending, distribution, accounta-
bility, political practice, over-bloated bureaucracies, large ministries, agencies and 
parastatals, and even the nature of federalism, are all built around a centrally redis-
tributed oil money model. As noted above, Obaseki considers that the federal gov-
ernment is ‘stuck in the past’ and needs to restructure the country in order to make 
progress (Shaibu 2024), since while the country is not earning enough to cover its 
expenditures, government spending is not decreasing.
However, this pattern of wasteful spending and aging political structure will be less 
and less the norm. Political institutions must readjust to reality with the downward 
trend from decarbonisation and new energy technologies shaping the transport sector

and reducing revenue. One practical argument is the proposal to regroup the existing 
36 states into six regions, at most eight, which will be the federating units (i.e. the levels 
of government). Aggregating states is needed to save costs from the wasteful duplica-
tion of consumption and recurrent spending. The regions will become federating units 
with decentralised fiscal federalism, leading to the reformation of centre–component 
unit relations that border on revenue allocation, for example, the rearrangement of 
the revenue allocation formula favouring lower-level governments and resource con-
trol, a return to pre-1966 fiscal relations that emphasised derivation. A possible political 
approach would be for the federating units to negotiate a new constitution with suffi-
cient regional autonomy. Restructuring the federation in this sense includes a return to 
the pre-1966 federal system, the 1960/63 Constitution (Egwim 2020, 39; Subair 2023).
Conclusion
Nigeria has had two eras featuring a mono-resource-based economy with aligned insti-
tutional and political structures. However, the current post-oil, taxed earnings economy 
cannot function with the oil economy’s underlying political structures and institutions: 
a prosperous post-oil economy will not work with the same legal and institutional foun-
dations designed for the consumption of oil rents. There is a need for state and regional 
governments to become financially self-sufficient units and drivers of the economy, 
rather than acting as a bar to development and to reform of the duplication of expenses 
throughout the current 36-state structure. Enhanced financial capabilities in the coun-
try’s regional structure were not implemented because of the oil boom, military rule 
and the creation of miniature states. But now, reductions in the oil revenue due to inter-
nal and global factors are at the centre of radical debates about reducing the number 
of states and reverting to larger-scale regional structures. Nigeria’s public finances are 
changing, and more revenues are being raised from non-oil sources. Irrespective of the 
wishes of individual actors, the force of the current Nigerian economic situation makes 
it imperative for a new political structure now to emerge.
