# Chapter 6 Statutory-Hooks Reference (verified)

Purpose: the verified legal instruments each Chapter 6 recommendation attaches to, mined against authoritative sources (the PIA arrangement of sections from the NUPRC-hosted gazette text; the NOGICD Act; the NUPRC divestment framework graphic; and NUPRC and news sources for the 2024-2025 transactions). This is drafting reference, not chapter prose. Two Chapter 5 corrections fell out of the mining and are flagged at the end.

Sources of verification: PIA 2021 arrangement of sections, official gazette text (ngfcp.nuprc.gov.ng searchable PIA); NOGICD Act 2010 (NCDMB and FAOLEX texts); NUPRC "Regulatory Divestment Framework" graphic (seven pillars, project-supplied); NUPRC press statements on the 2023 regulations and the 2024-2025 divestment approvals; transaction reporting (Shell, NUPRC, trade press), June 2026.

## A. The corrections this mining surfaced (act on before locking Chapter 5 and drafting 6.3)

1. **PIA Section 117 is the wrong hook. The correct hook is PIA Section 95.**
   - **Section 95** = "Assignments, mergers, transfers and acquisitions," in Chapter 2, Part II (Administration of **Upstream** Petroleum Operations). This governs assignment of an interest in a petroleum prospecting licence or petroleum mining lease, that is, the IOC upstream divestments the thesis studies (OMLs/PMLs). Minister's consent on the recommendation of the Commission, per Section 3(1)(h).
   - **Section 117** = "Assignment or transfer of licence or permit," in Chapter 2, Part III (Administration of **Midstream and Downstream** Petroleum Operations). It governs transfer of a midstream/downstream licence or permit (pipelines, gas processing, refining), not an upstream lease.
   - Chapter 5 cites "PIA Section 117" for the upstream consent hook in roughly four places (Sections 5.3.x, 5.4, 5.5, Table 5.3/5.4). Each should become **Section 95**. Section 117 remains citable only where a transaction's midstream components (for example a gas-processing plant transferred alongside the leases) are specifically in view.

2. **PIA Section 104 is a dead end.** Section 104 = "Gas flaring penalties." It has nothing to do with capability, consent, or transfer. The reviewers' suggested "Section 104" citation should not be used anywhere. (Reviewers had explicitly flagged 117 and 104 as "to be confirmed, not assumed"; both are now resolved, 117 wrong-subsector, 104 wrong-subject.)

3. **Framework-name caveat (verify or describe functionally).** Chapter 5 names the NUPRC consent test the "Upstream Asset Divestment and Exit Guidance Framework." That exact title was not confirmed against an authoritative NUPRC source in this pass. NUPRC's own graphic titles it the **"Regulatory Divestment Framework,"** and NUPRC press statements describe it functionally as the seven-pillar assessment applied at consent under the **Petroleum (Assignment of Interest) Regulations 2023**. Safest fix: either confirm the exact official title from an NUPRC source, or describe it functionally ("NUPRC's seven-pillar divestment assessment, applied at consent under the Petroleum (Assignment of Interest) Regulations 2023") rather than asserting a possibly-unofficial name.

## B. Verified hooks, per recommendation (the 6.3 sequence)

**Recommendation 1, asset-specific disclosure and crisis-readiness as a consent condition.**
- **PIA Section 95** (assignment of an upstream interest requires Minister's consent on the Commission's recommendation; the Commission attaches conditions).
- **PIA Section 3(1)(h)** (the Minister's power to assign interests "upon the recommendation of the Commission and pursuant to the regulations").
- **Petroleum (Assignment of Interest) Regulations 2023** (elevated the former DPR/NUPRC divestment guidelines to a regulation; houses the technical-capacity consent criterion the thesis calls "Reg. 7"). Reg. 7 exact subsection letters: residual, pin from the regulation text; the substantive criterion, demonstrated technical capacity at consent, is confirmed.
- **NUPRC seven-pillar divestment framework, Pillar 1 (Technical Capacity):** "proven and verifiable capacity to operate the asset ... competencies and capabilities comparable to or surpassing those of the divesting entity." This is corporate-comparator language; the thesis re-specifies it to asset-specific crisis readiness. This pillar is the single most important positioning anchor for the chapter.

**Recommendation 2, continuity-of-operating-knowledge terms in the assignment agreement.**
- **NOGICD Act Section 31** (succession plan / understudy: the operator submits a succession plan and Nigerians understudy each incumbent expatriate for up to four years). This is the existing knowledge-transfer-by-shadowing mechanism; the thesis re-specifies it from expatriate-to-Nigerian succession toward seller-to-buyer operating-knowledge transfer at the point of divestment.
- **PIA Section 95** (the assignment terms in which the seller's transition obligations, retention/secondment, data and process-knowledge transfer, understudy period, are made mandatory rather than negotiated).
- Cross-reference NOGICD **Sections 43-47** (research and development / technology transfer) only as supporting context; the literature treats them as exhortatory, which strengthens, not weakens, the argument that a binding transfer obligation is needed.

**Recommendation 3, demonstrated buyer absorptive capacity, with a qualified technical operator for Tier 1 cases.**
- **PIA Section 95** + **Petroleum (Assignment of Interest) Regulations 2023, technical-capacity criterion** (consent conditioned on demonstrated absorptive capacity).
- **NOGICD Sections 7-9** (Nigerian Content Plan: first consideration, submission, and Board public review) and the technical-operator route, the mechanism by which a de novo buyer supplies operating depth through a qualified technical operator or partner.
- Framework Pillar 1 (Technical Capacity) again, re-specified to asset-specific.

**Recommendation 4, staged operatorship for high-risk transfers.**
- **PIA Section 95** (the Commission's power to attach conditions to consent; staged operatorship is a consent condition, not a new instrument).
- **PIA Section 7(d)** (the Commission "administer, monitor and enforce compliance with the terms and conditions of leases and licences"), the standing authority under which staged conditions are supervised.

**Recommendation 5, post-transfer verification tied to rule-based triggers.**
- **PIA Section 7(d) and 7(g)** (the Commission's technical regulatory functions: monitor and enforce lease/licence conditions; "carry out enquiries, tests, audits or investigations and take other steps to monitor the activities of licensees"). This is the existing standing power the thesis re-specifies into scheduled, rule-based post-transfer verification.
- **PIA Section 231** (power to issue administrative penalties) as the rule-based-consequence anchor; the triggers connect integrity findings to graduated, pre-defined consequences rather than to discretionary action.

**Recommendation 6, financial security and community-readiness measures.**
- **PIA Sections 232-233** (Abandonment, decommissioning and disposal; the Decommissioning and Abandonment Fund). Confirmed; full responsibility for decommissioning sits with the licensee/lessee, and the Fund must be set up under the Upstream Petroleum Decommissioning and Abandonment Regulations. This is Pillar 4 (DANDA).
- **PIA Section 103** (financial contribution for remediation of environmental damage; the Environmental Remediation Fund). Part of Pillar 5.
- **PIA Chapter 3, Sections 234-257, Host Communities Development** (Section 235 incorporation of the Host Communities Development Trust; **Section 240(2) the funding obligation**, an annual contribution equal to three percent of the settlor's actual annual operating expenditure of the preceding financial year, verified June 2026 against the gazetted PIA text (Section 240, titled "Sources of funding for petroleum host communities development trust") and NUPRC statements; Section 251 host-communities needs assessment; Section 252 host-communities development plan). This is the community-readiness hook and Pillar 5. Correction: an earlier version of this line attributed the funding obligation to Section 257; the three-percent contribution is Section 240(2). Section 257 is the vandalism, sabotage, and civil-unrest deduction clause, under which the cost of repairing damaged facilities is excluded from the fund, confirmed against the gazetted text, not a funding provision.

## C. The NUPRC seven-pillar divestment framework (primary source, project graphic)

NUPRC assesses these at consent (corporate-level; the thesis's re-specification target is Pillar 1):
1. **Technical Capacity**, proven, verifiable capacity to operate the asset; competencies comparable to or surpassing the divesting entity; unitisation/straddled-field issues resolved.
2. **Financial**, balance sheet and financial viability; readiness for a defined work programme; due diligence on suitability, reputation, track record.
3. **Legal**, "fit and proper" acquirer; legacy debts and encumbrances resolved; residuals managed.
4. **Decommissioning and Abandonment (DANDA)**, applicable D&A costs assessed and settled; Nigerian government exposure averted (PIA Sections 232-233).
5. **Host Community Trust / Environmental Remediation Fund**, HCDT obligations and social-inclusion robustness per the PIA; decarbonisation and ESG (PIA Section 103, Sections 234-257).
6. **Industrial Relations and Labour Issues**, assessment mechanism to avert labour disharmony; "Certificate of Settlement" on staff welfare, benefits, disengagement, redundancies.
7. **Data Repatriation**, all data mined over the asset's life repatriated to the National Data Repository.

Positioning use: the seven pillars show the regulator already screens at consent, which makes the thesis's recommendations a re-specification of an existing route, not a parallel track. What the pillars do not do, and what the framework adds: asset-specific crisis-readiness verification (Pillar 1 is corporate-comparator), a binding seller-to-buyer operating-knowledge transfer obligation, and post-transfer verification (the pillars are assessed once, at consent).

## D. Tier 2, post-2024 transactions (positioning only; future tests, never present validation)

- **Renaissance Africa Energy / SPDC:** completed 13 March 2025, USD 2.4 billion, 100% of SPDC (the 30% SPDC-JV stake). NUPRC initially rejected the transaction in August 2024 ("could not scale the regulatory test"; consortium reported "unqualified" for the large onshore portfolio), approved December 2024, completed March 2025. Production reported above 200,000 bpd by mid-2025 and gas output around 2.2 bcf by early 2026. Consortium: ND Western, Aradel Holdings, Petrolin, FIRST E&P, Waltersmith. Use: the initial rejection is direct evidence that consent-stage capability screening is feasible and already exercised; the production figures are an early continuity signal under the newer regime, a future outcome test, not evidence supporting the thesis now.
- **Oando / NAOC (Eni):** approved 2024, ~USD 783 million; OMLs 60, 61, 62, 63 and two gas plants; NUPRC evaluated technical and financial capacity with decommissioning liabilities secured upfront.
- **Seplat / MPNU (ExxonMobil):** approved late 2024; NUPRC confirmed operational readiness and secured over USD 400 million in pre-sale decommissioning liabilities; Seplat output nearly tripled in early 2025.
- **Chappal Energies / Equinor:** ministerial consent August 2024. **Telema Energies / TotalEnergies:** approved (recent).

Framing rule for 6.5: as these mature under the newer regulatory environment, they can test whether the framework's logic holds; they do not validate it now (consistent with the Renaissance design-contrast treatment in Chapters 1, 3, and 5).
