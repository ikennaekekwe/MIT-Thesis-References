INTRODUCTION 
This paper will examine some reported cases of environmental degradation in Niger Delta as a 
result of oil and gas production activities. It will consider the extent of oil pollution to the 
groundwater, vegetation, aquatic animals, farmlands and human health in the region. Also, it will 
identify some weaknesses in existing legislations, failure of specialized agencies to implement 
the laws and how these issues directly affect the inhabitants of Niger Delta. The Chapter will 
also examine the access of the victims of oil pollution to court in claims for compensation.

Challenges of access to compensation by victims of oil and gas pollution in the Niger Delta 
This segment shall focus on the analysis of the claims for compensation by victims of oil 
pollution in Niger Delta. Oftentimes, after the occurrence of oil spillage or flared gas the 
government and oil companies are concerned with remediation effort for the soil without taking 
into consideration the health and wellbeing of the indigenous people living within the precinct of 
the polluted environment.  The indigenous people usually resort to litigation9 in order to seek 
reliefs for destruction of their farms, vegetation, underground water, streams, cultural objects 
inter alia. Thus, this segment considers some of the decided cases affirming the rights of the 
victims to reasonable compensation. First, it is important to ascertain who can sue in claims for 
compensation for oil and gas pollution as presented below.

victims of oil and gas pollution in Niger Delta

The Supreme Court was called upon to interpret the foregoing provisions in the case of Attorney 
General of the Federation v Attorney General of Abia & 35 Ors24. In that case, the Supreme 
Court gave the entire ownership and control of petroleum resources to the federal government 
exclusively. Thus, it is a settled law that the ownership of petroleum resources is vested in the 
Nigerian Government.  
The foregoing analysis is necessary to lay the foundation for the locus standi of the victims of oil 
gas pollution to seek redress in court for exploitation of their lands resulting in oil pollution. 
Though the ownership is vested in the government, the citizens have right to the surface of the 
land and where the land is polluted; they can sue the violators to seek redress.  
It is unarguable that oil is enclosed in the land, and thus, land acquisition is a necessary 
precondition for oil operation. In general, lawyers especially of the common law tradition 
understand land to include minerals enclosed in the land, although the state may reserve its 
ownership to itself25. Therefore, with regards to oil operations, although oil is vested in the state, 
the oil bearing/landowning communities/villages or families do not participate in granting 
concessions to the oil prospecting and production companies, the companies could only enter the 
affected land only after reaching an agreement with landowning communities/villages or families 
on the amount of compensation to be paid to them for any damage to surface rights and 
compensation as an annual rent. Under the native land tenure system, land is connected with 
minerals entrapped in it. Thus, minerals, buildings, trees and other fixtures on land form part of 
the land and belong to the owner of the land. Thus, if anything is affixed to the land without 
expenditure of labour, it belongs to the owner of the land.

Suffice to say the victims of oil pollution can sue oil companies and claim damages for 
destruction of crops, land, drinking water, cultural objects, amongst other. This was the decision 
of the court in the following cases. In the case of Shell v. Farah26, several families sued Shell for 
compensation for a well blow-out. The court established that Shell's evidence was not reliable 
and the plaintiffs were awarded N4, 621,307 in compensation. Dissatisfied with the judgment, 
Shell appealed against the decision. The Court of Appeal confirmed the award in favor of the 
victims. In this case, plaintiffs were awarded compensation under multiple heads including the 
loss of income, the social effect/general inconvenience and the rehabilitation of the land. Before 
the Farah case arose, Shell had paid compensation to the community for the crops damaged as a 
result of oil operations. But the court found that the victims should also have been compensated 
for the loss of income. In the course of the two decades after the accident, the victims were 
unable to use the land for farming. On that basis, they were awarded N2, 371,307 for the loss of 
income for the period of 19 years. Since Shell failed to rehabilitate the land that time, the 
community was also awarded two million naira for the rehabilitation of the land. 
In the case of Fufeyin v. Shell-BP27  the plaintiffs were awarded N5, 569.00 for the destruction of 
houses, crops, fishing creeks, canals, fish ponds, traps and other property because of oil 
pollution. Similarly, in the case of Shell v. Isaiah,28 N22, 000,000.00 was awarded as 
compensation against Shell-BP.

Choice of appropriate jurisdiction for litigation by victims of oil pollution 
It is pertinent for the victims of oil pollution to approach the appropriate court in the process of 
seeking for compensation. Filing a suit at the wrong court may be fatal to the success of the case. 
This was exemplified in the case of Kiobel v. Royal Dutch Petroleum Co29 the plaintiffs claimed 
that Royal Dutch Shell compelled Shell Nigeria its Nigerian subsidiary, in cooperation with the 
Nigerian government, in a brutal crushing of peaceful resistance to aggressive oil development in 
Ogoni, Niger Delta. The District Court dismissed the plaintiffs' claims for aiding and abetting 
property destruction; forced exile; extrajudicial killing; and violation of the rights to life, liberty, 
security, and association. The mater went to Court of Appeal. The U.S. Court of Appeals for the 
Second Circuit held that corporations cannot be held liable for violations of customary 
international law. The case was further appealed to the U.S Supreme Court and it found that it 
would be implausible to suppose that the First Congress wanted to make the United States a 
uniquely hospitable forum for the enforcement of international norms. 
The reason for the dismissal of this suit was not based on evidence of the case but the focus was 
on the jurisdiction of the court to entertain matters arising from foreign jurisdiction. This 
argument would have been avoided if the suit was filed in The Hague (Netherlands) where the 
parent company (Shell-BP) is domiciled. Thus, is very important to consider the jurisdiction of 
courts while filing suits for compensation.

Similarly, in the case of Shell Petroleum Development Company of Nigeria Limited v. Sirpi-
Alusteel Construction Limited30 where the Court of Appeal was called upon to determine 
jurisdiction of the High Court to entertain matters touching on natural sources. The Court 
of Appeal Per Galadima J.C.A held thus; 
The Federal High Court, like the High Court of a State, is a creature of the 
Constitution and its jurisdiction is defined by the relevant laws. The Court below 
was referred to S. 251 (1)(n) of the 1999 Constitution and section 7(1) and 7(3) of 
the Federal High Court Act. Section 251(1)(n) of the Constitution provides that the 
Federal High Court shall have and exercise exclusive jurisdiction in “mines and 
minerals (including oil fields, oil mining, geological surveys and natural gas)”. 
Section 7(1)(n) of the Federal High Court Act is similarly worded as S. 251(1)(n) 
of the Constitution while S. 7(3) stipulates that S. 7(1)(n) shall be construed to 
include jurisdiction to hear and determine all issues relating to, arising from or 
ancillary to mines and minerals31. In other words, the jurisdiction of a court to 
adjudicate on a matter is predicated upon the facts placed before it and more 
importantly by the phraseology of the plaintiff‟s claim. 
 
According to the decision in this case, the appropriate court (though not the only court) to 
approach while filing suits for compensation as a result of oil and gas pollution is the Federal 
High Court a provided in Section 251 (1)(n) of the 1999 Constitution and Section 7(1) and 7(3) 
of the Federal High Court Act respectively. However, the High Court may assume jurisdiction in 
certain circumstance as shown in the earlier cited case of Kiobel v. Royal Dutch Petroleum Co32 
where the matter was instituted in High Court and not the Federal High Court. In sum, both the 
High Court and Federal High Court have jurisdiction in deserving circumstances.33
