A B S T R A C T
An increasingly common form of organizational change in high-hazard industries is the acquisition of ageing 
facilities. While the safety implications of other types of organizational change have been widely studied, the 
risks associated with ageing facility acquisitions remain under-investigated. This acquisition process is often 
accompanied by significant challenges in interorganizational knowledge transfer (IKT). Although knowledge is 
widely recognized as essential to process safety, the impacts of IKT failures require further exploration, especially 
when original operational teams are not retained. This study aims to identify the process safety impacts of un­
successful IKT during the acquisition of an ageing facility in which no personnel were transferred. Drawing on a 
qualitative case study of an offshore oil and gas platform acquisition, this study offers an in-depth analysis of the 
organizational and operational discontinuities that emerged during the asset handover. Safety incident data from 
the case study platform revealed an increase in safety incidents following the acquisition. Interviews with process 
safety experts and practitioners were conducted to map IKT challenges to Risk-Based Process Safety (RBPS). 
Failures in IKT were found to vary in their impact on process safety. Governance-related aspects of the IKT 
process, such as the availability of personnel from both companies during the transfer, and access to legacy 
databases, were found to be the most critical. These failures not only directly impacted RBPS elements such as 
process knowledge management but also contributed to safety impacts. These findings support the development 
of improved IKT frameworks for managers and regulators to avoid operational safety risks following an ageing 
facility acquisition.
1. Introduction
Six months after taking over operations of an ageing oil and gas 
offshore facility with a known background of recurring integrity issues, 
the new operator was unable to clearly explain the causes behind a 
critical operational failure. The company attributed the incident to 
ongoing diagnostic efforts. During subsequent oversight interactions, 
company representatives cited inherited practices and undocumented 
infrastructure conditions, stating: “We are still understanding how the 
system works,” “This was a legacy from the previous operator,” “We are 
discovering failures as we learn the plant,” and “We are still working to 
identify the systems and equipment listed as critical for this unit.” These are 
quotes statements that reflect the operational uncertainty and point to 
deeper systemic vulnerabilities that often accompany the acquisition of 
ageing assets.

context in which work is performed, impact staff competencies, shift 
decision-making processes, and unsettle power distribution, potentially 
undermining process safety. Organizational change is a broad term that 
encompasses alterations in working conditions, personnel changes, task 
allocation modifications, organizational restructuring, and policy 
changes (CCPS, 2013).
Several accidents in high-hazard facilities have been connected to 
the improper management of organizational changes of various types. 
For example, the Esso Longford explosion of 1998 was connected to a 
loss of personnel, the Texas City Refinery Explosion of 2005 was related 
to cost-cutting, and the Hertfordshire Oil Storage Terminal Fire of 2005 
was related to the introduction of new operating systems (Energy 
Institute, 2020). Deficient change management was also found to be one 
of the causal factors of the 2015 explosion on the Floating Production, 
Storage and Offloading (FPSO) facility Cidade de S˜ao Mateus. One of the 
largest accidents in the oil and gas industry in Brazil, this explosion led 
to 9 fatalities and left 26 injured (ANP, 2015). This facility had been 
acquired and transferred between operating companies. Despite the 
potential adverse consequences of facility acquisitions, the process 
safety impacts of this scenario are still under-investigated in the litera­
ture and are not considered by the Center for Chemical Process Safety 
CCPS guidelines (CCPS, 2013).
In the context of facility acquisitions, one case has become particu­
larly relevant: the transfer of ageing platforms between companies. The 
acquisition of ageing assets has become an increasingly common form of 
organizational change, especially for oil and gas facilities sold from 
larger to smaller companies (Barndt et al., 2000; Ferreira et al., 2023). In 
many instances, no personnel are transferred to the acquiring company. 
The complete workforce change makes interorganizational knowledge 
transfer (IKT) — the process of transferring knowledge between inde­
pendent organizations — especially challenging in this context. After all, 
the tacit knowledge held by long-standing operational teams—crucial 
for safely managing potentially degraded, obsolete, and poorly docu­
mented systems within the ageing facility—is often difficult to transfer 
when key individuals are lost during the asset acquisition process. When 
poorly executed, IKT can result in limited understanding of 
facility-specific risks, subjective interpretation of safety guidelines, and 
ultimately, an underestimated safety risk for operations. Despite its 
relevance, the process safety impacts arising from potential failures in 
IKT during ageing facility acquisition remain an under-explored topic in 
the academic literature and largely absent from existing regulatory 
frameworks (Health and Safety Executive - HSE, 2014; Norwegian Oil 
and Gas Association, 2017).
The qualitative case study herein explored investigates how chal­
lenges in interorganizational knowledge transfer (IKT) affect process 
safety during the handover of ageing offshore oil and gas assets. The 
focus on the safety implications of unsuccessful knowledge transfer 
during ageing asset handover is novel and provides insights into how 
latent organizational conditions develop and persist in high-risk in­
dustries, particularly in the context of ageing infrastructures.
Given the context of IKT in the acquisition of ageing facilities without 
personnel transfer, along with the previously mentioned gaps in the 
literature and regulations, the present study addresses the following 
research question: What are the process safety impacts of an unsuccessful 
interorganizational knowledge transfer during the acquisition of an ageing 
asset?
To answer this research question, Section 2 of the present study starts 
by reviewing the body of knowledge and noting the gaps in previous 
process safety and knowledge transfer literature. Section 3 describes the 
case study, and the abductive reasoning approach selected. Incident data 
from the case study platform was analysed, and expert interviews were 
conducted to identify the impacts of an unsuccessful IKT. Section 4 be­
gins by presenting the findings on safety incidents involving the case 
study platform, both prior to and following its acquisition. Then, the 
results of the qualitative analysis connecting potential IKT challenges to 
process safety impacts are presented. Implications of these findings are 
discussed in Section 5 and overall conclusions in Section 6.
2. Theoretical background
2.1. Organizational change and process safety
Whereas earlier studies and guidelines have predominantly 
addressed the safety implications of technical changes, research has 
demonstrated that organizational changes can significantly impact 
safety too (Zwetsloot et al., 2007). As change usually involves both 
technical and organizational changes, these should be considered in an 
integrated way (Gerbec, 2017). Graamans et al. (2020) exemplified this 
point, arguing that even switching material suppliers can have organi­
zational implications because the use of the material can be embedded in 
a social arrangement in which individuals assign meaning to that ma­
terial and how it contributes to risk avoidance.
To avoid safety incidents caused by poorly managed changes, pre­
vious studies have developed tools for changes of various sizes, from 
mitigating risks related to day-to-day change (Gerbec, 2017) to 
addressing major changes, including identifying the organizational 
factors that lead to safe changes (Grote, 2008), evaluating organiza­
tional readiness for change (Levovnik and Gerbec, 2018), investigating 
systems perspectives in organisational change (Nolan-McSweeney et al., 
2023), and identifying the challenges caused by the changes (Zwetsloot 
et al., 2007). However, these studies have not considered the organi­
zational change scenario of facility acquisition, where no personnel are 
transferred to the acquiring company, revealing a need for further 
research.
Additionally, organizational change has clearly been linked to pro­
cess safety challenges; nevertheless, it remains to be investigated what 
elements of process safety are the most impacted, and which organiza­
tional factors most significantly affect process safety in such scenarios. 
Specific organizational influences can be difficult to detect because they 
may not directly cause an accident, but create cascading effects on un­
safe supervision, internal and external conditions, and worker actions 
(Wang et al., 2024). At a high level, Zwetsloot et al. (2014) observed that 
operator competence and participative leadership were key contributors 
to safety disturbances.
The importance of knowledge and competence in managing process 
safety has also been highlighted in industry guidelines, as evidenced by 
the Center for Chemical Process Safety (CCPS) framework for managing 
risk-based process safety (RBPS). This is a well-established framework 
and is applicable to all process industries, i.e., facilities handling haz­
ardous materials. It outlines 20 elements distributed among four RBPS 
pillars, namely: 1) Commit to process safety, 2) Understand hazard and 
risk, 3) Manage risk, and 4) Learn from experience (CCPS, 2007). The 
elements of each pillar are listed in Table 1. Two elements are clearly 
related to knowledge and competence, i.e., process safety competency, 
part of the pillar “commit to process safety”, and process knowledge 
management, in the pillar “understand hazard and risk”.
The element of process safety competency involves developing, 
increasing, and supporting the application of the body of knowledge that 
helps individuals at all levels of an organization to make proper risk- 
based decisions (CCPS, 2015). Process safety competency encompasses 
a combination of knowledge, skills, and attitude, with process safety 
culture shaping this attitude by fostering group values and behaviors 
that influence how process safety is managed. Thus, according to CCPS 
(2015), the elements of process safety culture, process knowledge 
management, and management of change influence or are influenced by 
the process safety competency element.
Process knowledge management involves the systematic retention, 
compilation and dissemination of a specific set of knowledge and critical 
information within the organization, such as through written technical 
documents and specifications, engineering drawings and calculations, 
specifications for design, fabrication, and installation of process

As explained by CCPS (2015), the process safety competency element 
complements the knowledge element by ensuring that individuals can 
properly understand, interpret and apply the information that is

the cornerstone of management of change (CCPS, 2013). After all, a

roles and responsibilities in relation to process safety, as well as a pro­

every individual (CCPS, 2013). In cases where significant or abrupt 
organizational changes occur, such as company acquisitions and staff 
turnover, effective and rapid knowledge dissemination becomes essen­
tial to ensure continuity of operations within a context of proper major 
accident risk management. Management of change is thus essential to 
knowledge management in these scenarios (Singh et al., 2010), as its 
lack has been shown to contribute to process safety incidents (CCPS, 
2013). Although the scenario of facility acquisition, the focus herein, is 
not considered in the CCPS guidelines (CCPS, 2013), it represents an 
extreme case of personnel turnover, and its safety implications should be 
further investigated.
The 2015 explosion in the pump room of FPSO Cidade de S˜ao 
Mateus, off the coast of Brazil, provides compelling evidence of how 
process safety can be compromised because of management of change 
failures during facility transfers (ANP, 2015). The FPSO was initially 
operated by Prosafe in 2006, under a contract with Petrobras, and in 
2010 sold to BWGroup (ANP, 2015). In 2011, the latter initiated the 
assessment, revision, and migration of documents to the BW manage­
ment systems. Onboard teams were responsible for evaluating the 
operational procedures, but no management of change was in place for 
this task, leading critical information and operational documents to be 
unavailable. For instance, until the date of the accident in 2015, the 
operating procedure of the cargo equipment had not been migrated and 
had a warning on its cover that stated the “procedure and its references are 
under development, read carefully and speak with your supervisor before 
use”.
The FPSO Cidade de S˜ao Mateus accident occurred while a cargo 
tank was being emptied. The event was triggered by a condensate leak 
caused by the inadvertent blocking of a positive displacement pump’s 
discharge. Information on the pump’s state was lacking in the Human- 
Machine Interface (HMI), which meant cargo operators had to infer 
the state of the pump by observing the percentage of opening of the 
steam control valve to the pump’s pistons. The operating procedure of 
the cargo systems had no guidance to support this operation, whose 
success depended on the experience and knowledge of the marine op­
erators (ANP, 2015). Furthermore, the team of marine operators of this 
FPSO had undergone a degradation process marked by the loss of 
experienced personnel and a reduction in staffing to below the minimum 
level, impacting the process safety competencies available onboard. This 
example provides us with insight into the potential impacts of the asset 
acquisition process without personnel migration to the acquiring com­
pany. The failure to update and revise documentation following the 
facility acquisition indicates an issue related to knowledge management. 
The fact that the acquiring company brought new personnel to the fa­
cility, with its own set of skills and attitude, is a further indication that 
knowledge is the key component of competency being transferred be­
tween companies. The next subsection further explores the element of 
knowledge management.
2.2. Knowledge management
Many reports on incidents in high-hazard industries have found that 
these could be avoided or minimized by enhancing knowledge (Lilburne 
et al., 2021). Garud et al. (2011) concluded that organizational memory, 
encompassing both explicit and tacit knowledge, was fundamental for 
effective decision-making and overall performance in asset manage­
ment. Tacit knowledge is developed over time and is not easily trans­
ferable. It requires a combination of training and other methods to be 
effectively conveyed. Because it is not easily acquired, the loss of tacit 
knowledge has been shown to be more harmful for organizations (Galan, 
2023).
Starbuck and Farjoun (2005) observed that incomplete learning 
processes and problematic leadership transition were among the deep 
causes of the Columbia disaster. Silva (2017) also found that the loss of 
technological knowledge was the cause of a major accident on an off­
loading vessel (FPSO). The ability to share and apply knowledge is thus 
key to safety (McDonald, 2006), particularly in high-risk settings. While 
the relevance of knowledge for accident prevention and adequate risk 
management is well acknowledged in the literature (Klein, 2012; Li 
et al., 2020), the potential failure in knowledge transfer represents an 
underestimated safety risk (Wu et al., 2013).
Knowledge transfer is the process of sharing and disseminating 
knowledge between individuals, groups or organizations. An unsuc­
cessful knowledge transfer leads not only to reduced benefits but also to 
significant losses, such as the erosion of knowledge due to staff turnover 
or discontinued collaborations (Argote et al., 2000), a decline in oper­
ational performance (Inkpen, 2008), or even threats to the health and 
safety of workers (Duryan et al., 2020; Huang and Yang, 2019). When 
knowledge transfer occurs across organizational boundaries, such as for 
facility acquisition, it is designated an interorganizational knowledge 
transfer (IKT).
Previous IKT studies have largely focused on other types of re­
lationships, such as alliances (Becerra et al., 2008; Cheung et al., 2011; 
Martinkenaite, 2011; Milagres and Burcharth, 2019; Schoenberg, 
2001a), company acquisitions (Martinkenaite, 2012; Schoenberg, 
2001b), mergers (Yoo et al., 2007), collaboration with supply chain 
(Whitehead et al., 2019) or academic collaborations (Chakraborty et al., 
2024), as the ability to transfer knowledge is context-dependent 
(Milagres and Burcharth, 2019). While (Ferreira et al., 2024) studied 
the elements of IKT in the context of facility acquisition, they did not 
consider its outcomes.
In the absence of more in-depth studies and research on the topic of 
IKT during facility acquisition, organizations may not be addressing 
potential issues related to process safety or may be adopting ineffective 
solutions. For instance, a shadowed operation is recommended by the 
Brazilian regulator and consists of a period during which the acquiring 
company’s team observes the asset operation by the selling company’s

2.3. Ageing assets
Facility transfer between operating companies is a common phe­
nomenon among ageing assets. In different geographic locations, larger 
oil and gas companies are increasingly moving away from the 
production-to-depletion paradigm to focus on more lucrative production 
areas. As a result, these larger companies tend to sell their ageing fa­
cilities to smaller operating companies (Ferreira et al., 2024). The 
migration of the operational teams to the acquiring facility is not the 
norm. For instance, from 2019 to 2022, nineteen offshore units were 
purchased by smaller companies in Brazil. None of these included the 
transfer of technical personnel between companies (ANP, 2019, ANP 
2020, ANP 2021, ANP 2022b ). Hence, this facility transfer process is not 
only marked by the challenges of organizational changes, as new 
personnel are operating the acquired facility, but it also brings the 
technical challenges related to ageing.
Ageing is a general process through which the characteristics of 
structures, systems, and components gradually change with time and 
use. The term is commonly applied to refer to changes that are, or at 
least could be, detrimental to safety(IAEA, 2018). In other words, the 
term “ageing” is usually applied interchangeably with ageing-related 
degradation (Monteiro, 2020). Degradation, in turn, can be defined as 
a process of immediate or gradual deterioration of structures, systems 
and components that can potentially impair their ability to function 
within acceptance criteria (IAEA, 2006).
In the Netherlands, between 2004 and 2018, 25 % of loss of 
containment accidents in major hazard companies were caused by 
ageing, specifically material degradation (Kooi et al., 2020). Despite the 
growing evidence of the safety impacts of ageing, there is no established 
policy for managing ageing assets. An analysis of 83 accidents caused by 
material degradation at major hazard chemical sites in the Netherlands 
found that 60 % of these were related to recoverable failures that were 
not identified due to invisibility (Hansler et al., 2022). Invisibility can 
stem from defects being missed during inspection, defects being con­
cealed by other assets or by being buried and inaccessible, errors during 
maintenance, inspections not being carried out in a timely manner or 
being omitted altogether.
These causes of invisibility highlight the fact that ageing is a socio- 
technical phenomenon and, as a result, the organization’s perfor­
mance in inspections and maintenance dictates the safety impact of 
material degradation. Indeed, Hokstad et al. (2010) framed the ageing 
process in three axes: material degradation, obsolescence, and organi­
zational issues. Not only can organizational issues make it more difficult 
to manage material degradation, but material degradation can also 
create operational complexities, which make it harder to manage. In the 
context of oil and gas facilities, operations can become more complex 
due to ageing and a long history of operations (Ancione et al., 2024; 
Ferreira et al., 2020).
The FPSO Cidade de S˜ao Mateus accident (ANP, 2015), previously 
discussed in subsection 2.1, also represents an example of an ageing 
facility, specifically from the perspective of material degradation. The 
initial design of the FPSO considered that, in case the platform was 
connected exclusively to gas wells, the liquid phase resulting from the 
gas processing (named condensate) should be treated and exported 
together with the produced gas through a gas export pipeline. However, 
during the design phase, a decision was made to store the condensate in 
the cargo tanks for subsequent offloading. No management of change 
process was in place to manage this modification. The presence of aro­
matic hydrocarbons in the condensate caused the corrosion of sealing 
gaskets in the cargo transfer system’s block valves. This deterioration 
restricted the transfer and drainage operations of the cargo tanks, 
requiring the installation of devices to minimize hazards, such as spades 
to isolate sections with internally leaking valves. The leak that triggered 
the 2015 accident occurred at a flanged joint where a spade had been 
installed months before but did not meet the pressure rating of the pipe. 
Thus, when the accident occurred, operations were not only involved in 
emptying the cargo tank but also performing corrective maintenance on 
the degraded valves of the cargo system.
The nuances of operating an ageing asset make tacit knowledge 
essential. As facilities age, they accumulate a history of modifications, 
undocumented adaptations, and degradation that is often known only to 
long-serving operational staff. These individuals hold tacit knowledge 
essential for understanding legacy systems, informal practices, and the 
rationale behind decisions made over the years. This is why employee 
turnover or change of ownership becomes even more complicated for an 
ageing asset (Eliopoulou et al., 2016).
Change in ownership has also been identified as a challenge for oil 
and gas facilities (Ferreira et al., 2024) particularly because the 
acquiring company is generally smaller (Barndt et al., 2000), and less 
mature in operational safety risk management. Thus, it is especially 
important to consider the intersection of ageing and facility acquisition.
3. Material and methods
This research adopts a qualitative case study design to examine the 
process safety impacts associated with an unsuccessful IKT process be­
tween two companies during the acquisition of an ageing facility. As 
emphasized by Monteiro (2020), this approach allows for the detailed 
analysis of a single case, and offers valuable insights when investigating 
poorly understood or novel issues. As Hopkins (2016) argues, findings 
from a single case may provide novel theoretical insights to help other 
organizations avoid similar failures.
Although case study findings are context-specific, the generalization 
of results remains possible. As emphasized by Ruddin (2006) in such 
cases the responsibility for generalizing shifts from the researcher to the 
reader—provided the study includes sufficient contextual detail to 
support informed reflection.
In addition to the case study approach, document analysis with 
expert and practitioner interviews were also adopted to identify and 
explain how the IKT impacts emerged when the asset and its associated 
documentation were transferred without the retention of personnel from 
the selling company. Because people remained in their original organi­
zations, the purchasing company brought professionals with their own 
set of skills and attitudes to operating the newly acquired facility. As a 
result, knowledge—the core component of competency, alongside skills 
and attitude—was significantly affected during the transfer process.
Given the exploratory nature of the study and the novelty of the 
phenomenon observed, the case study approach (O’Leary, 2005) was 
combined with abductive reasoning. The latter is applied when the 
researched phenomenon is perceived as an anomaly and follows four 
steps: 1) observe the anomaly, (2) confirm the anomaly, (3) generate 
explanations, (4) evaluate explanations (Sætre and Van De Ven, 2021). 
An anomaly is defined by Sætre & Van De Ven (2021) as a new or 
unanticipated phenomenon that cannot be explained or is poorly un­
derstood in existing literature. In this study, the anomaly in question is 
the process safety impact of an unsuccessful inter-organizational 
knowledge transfer (IKT) during the acquisition of an ageing facility. 
While previous incidents, such as the FPSO Cidade de S˜ao Mateus ex­
plosion (ANP, 2015), revealed process safety consequences following 
asset acquisitions, the specific role of knowledge transfer in these

3.1. Research context and case study selection
The selected case study considers an ageing offshore production fa­
cility referred to herein as “Platform X”. This facility began operations in 
2006 and was slated to operate until 2025. Platform X was initially 
owned by Company A (the knowledge source) and was acquired by 
Company B (the recipient) with the intention to extend the platform’s 
operation. Both companies are based in Brazil, with Company A being 
markedly larger than Company B in all accounts, such as the number of 
operating facilities, production volume, and number of employees, 
among others. No personnel were transferred from Company A to 
Company B, which is common practice for Company A. The ownership 
transition lasted six months. Company B overtook the sole operation of 
the platform in January 2023 (“Day 1”).
Platform X was selected because it not only represents the case of an 
acquired ageing facility, but also because it occurred recently, meaning 
that safety data was available for the facility before and after the tran­
sition, and it was known that the transition involved IKT challenges.
The failures of knowledge transfer between these companies were 
examined by Ferreira (2024) within two distinct environments – 
onshore and offshore. These two environments differ from each other 
not only in terms of location but also in their roles and organizational 
structures during the asset transition. While the offshore environment 
comprised the operation of Platform X, i.e., extraction and processing of 
hydrocarbons from the seabed, the onshore environment comprised the 
corporate and support functions associated with offshore operations. In 
the onshore environment, both companies organized around specific 
“Work Streams” (WS) for knowledge transfer, such as Health, Safety and 
Environment (HSE), Subsea, Field Operations, etc. Each WS had desig­
nated leaders from both companies guiding the transfer. In upper-level 
management, each company appointed transition managers (TM), and 
a steering committee. In the offshore environment, the operational 
structure of Company A remained unchanged during the transition. 
Company B’s role was limited to that of an observer during the 
six-month transition period, a phase referred to as “shadowed opera­
tion”. During this time, Company B’s employees observed the operations 
conducted by Company A’s staff.
Although many of the challenges identified were observed in both 
the onshore and offshore environments, they often stemmed from 
distinct root causes specific to each context. In the onshore environment, 
challenges were predominantly related to the structure and governance 
of the knowledge transfer process. Examples include the lack of a dedi­
cated team for knowledge transfer, the misalignment between contractual 
provisions and operational requirements, and the prevalence of generic 
statements with poorly defined responsibilities and deadlines. These issues 
often resulted in ambiguity, delays, and uncoordinated handovers across 
critical support functions.
In contrast, in the offshore environment, the challenges were more 
behavioral and operational in nature. These included an ad-hoc approach 
to conflict resolution, competition between the platform’s operational prior­
ities and the demands of knowledge transfer, and an unwillingness to share 
knowledge stemming from resistance to the acquisition itself. While Com­
pany B was formally present during the transition period in a “shadowed 
operation” capacity, its ability to access and internalize operational 
knowledge was constrained by informal barriers and limited engage­
ment by the outgoing team.

3.2. Data collection to confirm safety impacts
To analyze the safety consequences of an ageing facility’s acquisition 
with unsuccessful IKT, incident data was collected for the case study 
facility before and after the asset transition. These were reported by 
Company A and by Company B to the Brazilian oil and gas regulator 
ANP, 2024), in accordance with Resolution ANP n◦882/2022 (ANP - 
Brazilian National Agency of Petroleum Natural Gas and Biofuels, 
2022a). The incident data was given in two major categories: accidents 
and near-incidents. A total of 90 incidents were reported by Company B 
between 2023 and 2024, while 218 incidents were reported by Company 
A between 2012 and 2023 (prior to the transfer of operations). Each 
incident is categorized by severity, being classified as major, moderate, 
or minor. This existing reporting and classification enabled a structured 
analysis of the safety performance before and after the asset transition, 
providi20ng a comparison between these two periods and bringing in­
sights into the potential impacts of the unsuccessful knowledge transfer 
on process safety.
Secondary data was also collected from Company B, including 
summaries of lessons learned from previous transition processes, as well 
as presentations made by both companies outlining the organizational 
structures during the transition. These documents were analysed to 
assess the alignment between actual transition actions and CCPS 
guidelines for managing organizational changes, as well as the actual 
availability of knowledge required to manage risks and minimize the 
impact on process safety.
3.3. Explanation generation
To generate explanations for the potential process safety impacts 
resulting from the acquisition of an ageing facility under unsuccessful 
IKT, key references were utilized to frame both unsuccessful IKT and 
process safety impacts. Specifically, IKT challenges identified in the 
research of Ferreira (2024) and Ferreira et al. (2024) were assessed as 
potential contributing factors to safety incidents, while RBPS elements 
were reviewed as potential impacts on process safety.
Ferreira et al. (2024) identified 27 IKT challenges in the offshore 
environment, while Ferreira (2024) identified 24 IKT challenges in the 
onshore environment, both using the same 22 key elements of IKT as a 
reference framework. Of these, 12 challenges overlapped, resulting in a 
total of 39 unique IKT challenges. These elements define critical aspects 
of IKT that must be managed during acquisitions of ageing facilities, 
including contractual characteristics, decision-making characteristics 
and conflict resolution, among others. In this study, these IKT elements 
were used to group corresponding process safety outcomes. The poten­
tial process safety outcomes are the 20 RBPS elements drawn from the 
Process Safety Management Framework developed by CCPS (CCPS, 
2007), as shown in Table 1. The relationship between each IKT element 
and its corresponding process safety impact was mapped through in­
terviews, as described in the following subsection.

After presenting each challenge, the expert was asked two questions: 
1) Do you think this knowledge transfer challenge could impact process 
safety? 2) If yes, how? Divergence rather than convergence was inten­
tionally sought in our method, as to capture a broad range of perspec­
tives and experiences from the experts, since the aim of the study was to 
identify and explore the complex and multifaceted challenges associated 
with IKT during oil and gas facility transitions. Given the limited liter­
ature on this topic, our goal was not to reach consensus but to uncover 
diverse insights and nuances that could inform future frameworks and 
practices. By using an open-ended approach, we enabled the experts to 
highlight unique challenges, contextual factors, and mechanisms that 
might otherwise be overlooked in a consensus-driven method, ensuring 
a richer and more comprehensive understanding of the issue.
The interview protocol was validated during the first interview, 
which served as a pilot to assess the clarity and relevance of the ques­
tions. Based on this initial experience, the one-on-one interview format 
was maintained throughout the study, as it aligned well with the 
research objectives and allowed for the collection of rich, detailed, and 
divergent perspectives from each participant. They allowed participants 
to provide quick feedback after each challenge and encouraged diver­
gent thinking. Consequently, one-on-one interviews were used 
throughout the data collection process. The interviews were conducted 
online with Microsoft Teams, with an average duration of 2 h, and were 
recorded with the permission of the interviewees. Interviews were 
transcribed electronically using Microsoft Teams. Manual notes were 
also taken during the interviews by the three interviewers regarding key 
points presented.
The process safety expert and practitioner interview transcripts un­
derwent a qualitative content analysis in two rounds, following the 
methodologies proposed by Schreier (2012) and Gioia et al. (2013). 
While the first round focused on the potential of IKT challenges to 
process safety, the second identified specific RBPS elements that would 
be impacted. The analyses focused on the empirical and technical ele­
ments of the participants’ description. The goal was to understand and 
identify possible impacts of the IKT challenges on process safety, based 
on participants’ perceptions and experience. All interview transcripts 
were manually treated using Microsoft Excel.
Prior to beginning the coding process, the transcript material was 
segmented, and a coding framework was developed in three distinct 
phases: selecting relevant content, determining segmentation criteria, 
and identifying coding units (Schreier, 2012). In the first content anal­
ysis round, each interview transcript served as the unit of analysis, with 
quotes deemed relevant if they contained statements reflecting the 
opinion of the specialist regarding the contribution/importance of the 
IKT challenges to impact on process safety. Following a thematic 
approach, IKT elements identified by Ferreira et al. (2024) served as the 
basis for the initial thematic structure. Building on this foundation, the 
coding process was developed to explore perceived impacts on process 
safety. These elements were marked across the interview data, with 
coding occurring simultaneously for all relevant segments. Through this 
process, key themes emerged inductively.
In the second round, a content analysis was conducted using data 
structure methods proposed by Gioia et al. (2013) to delineate the 
connections between research information and process safety theory. 
The quotes for each IKT element were deductively assigned to relevant 
RBPS elements, as established by (CCPS, 2007) (theory centered). Sim­
ilarities in the process safety experts’ discourse were used to confirm the 
most relevant associated RBPS elements for each IKT element.

approach. This due diligence information could include plant diagnosis, 
as well as the main differences and gaps between the two companies, 
enabling a better understanding of hazards and risks.
5. Discussion
Despite having experience operating other offshore assets, personnel 
from the buying company are inherently unfamiliar with the specific­
ities of the newly acquired field and ageing facility, as well as their 
implications on operations. The most critical IKT elements for process 
safety identified in the findings show the importance of well-defining 
governance mechanisms for IKT to ensure the transfer of knowledge 
occurs smoothly. Otherwise, challenges related to critical IKT elements 
can lead to significant safety impacts with cascading effects, causing 
more severe accidents, such as observed following the transition of 
platform X. Well-established governance mechanisms, such as the 
availability of professionals and access to databases, prevent resistance 
from both companies’ personnel in transmitting and absorbing 
knowledge and facilitate the unrestricted transfer of data and informa­
tion, which are essential for safely operating an ageing asset in the 
future.
If people are not available for the transfer (either because they were 
not hired in advance of the transition or because they are not engaged or 
reveal resistance to the process), knowledge transfer is compromised 
(Kang and Kim, 2017), and tacit knowledge is lost (Sudhindra et al., 
2017). For an ageing facility or asset, as the case presented herein, the 
overreliance on explicit knowledge gathered through reading of tech­
nical documents and procedures is especially problematic. This is 
because explicit knowledge largely reflects the facility’s initial state, i.e., 
“as good as new”, creating a gap between the assumed and actual state. 
This gap leaves the buyer unprepared to safely operate an ageing asset. It 
also highlights the infrequent updating of documentation to reflect the 
current condition of the facility—an important issue in the management 
of ageing assets, whether acquired or not.
Knowledge gaps (both tacit and explicit) introduce uncertainties in 
facility operations, making it challenging or even unfeasible (depending 
on the size and type of gap) to identify hazards and analyze risks. This, in 
turn, makes it difficult to manage facility risks, which could lead to 
process safety incidents (i.e., loss of containment), such as those regis­
tered on the Platform X after the acquisition. While personnel from the 
buyer could potentially learn “from the asset itself” starting from Day 1, 
operation through trial and error is more likely to lead to safety in­
cidents, as seen by the marked increased in near-misses following the 
transition of the facility. A trial-and-error approach is characterised by 
an unawareness of the background of the facility (degradation, perfor­
mance, construction, maintenance) and brings vulnerabilities. By 
“experimenting with the facility”, the buyer could make suboptimal 
decisions that would have been clearly misguided to the seller. 
Furthermore, operating under unexpected situations becomes the new 
status quo; resulting in less streamlined and efficient operations. In such 
cases, professionals would likely expend more energy in managing op­
erations, becoming more stressed and fatigued, which could hinder their 
ability to respond effectively to unexpected or emergency situations.
Good governance during the IKT is also key to addressing underlying 
knowledge management issues and formalizing other essential elements 
for effective IKT (Kang and Kim, 2017). These include less critical and 
yet manageable aspects, such as clear objectives and scope, routines 
between the parties, appropriate mechanisms. Additionally, it can 
identify the need for corrective actions to redefine strategies during the 
transfer, if necessary. Without good governance, the distinction that was 
drawn by experts between critical and manageable IKT elements is no 
longer relevant, as even manageable elements would not be managed 
and equally impact process safety.
The findings also point to a gap in the application of the RBPS 
Management of Change approach to the case of facility acquisitions. 
Applying this approach for improving the IKT planning would likely 
reduce the loss of relevant information and data needed for future op­
erations, while also ensuring clarity for professionals from both orga­
nizations. To improve IKT, other transfer mechanisms beyond 
shadowing should also be considered to ensure the buying company is 
able to safely operate the facility under various scenarios. For instance, 
complementary IKT methods could be defined based on the tacit nature 
of knowledge being shared, function type, and professionals’ preferred 
learning styles. This could involve workshops, theoretical classes, virtual 
reality and 3D unit simulations, joint reading of technical documents, 
etc., in addition to shadowing. Similarly, performance assurance 
mechanisms could be collaboratively designed to leverage the selling 
company’s expertise. Their professionals could support evaluating and 
mentoring personnel from the purchasing company.

hazard and risk” pillar. Not all IKT elements affected process safety 
equally. Based on their impact, elements were classified into four cate­
gories: (1) Most critical (e.g. contractual characteristics, decision mak­
ing characteristics); (2) Less critical (e.g. degree of similarity, 
identification of training needs); (3) Facilitators or hinderers of knowl­
edge transfer (e.g. alignment of the two companies in all working en­
vironments, and previous experience); and (4) Derivatives of other 
challenges (e.g. conflict resolution and training effectiveness).
The present study thereby advances the process safety literature by 
establishing which process safety pillars and elements are impacted by 
IKT failures during the acquisition of an ageing asset without personnel 
transfer. It adds that governance mechanisms are critical to maintaining 
safety in high-hazard facilities during transitions. Furthermore, it con­
tributes to the IKT body of knowledge by classifying IKT elements ac­
cording to their criticality for process safety and identifying the most 
essential for the acquiring company to prioritize. For the ageing asset 
literature, the study emphasizes the need to consider knowledge transfer 
(not only material degradation) as a key factor in asset management 
decisions.
Practice-wise, the study underscores that IKT must be an explicit 
commitment in facility transfer agreements. Contracts should recognize 
that what is being transferred is not only the physical infrastructure but 
also operational knowledge, which is both explicit (data) and tacit (held 
by personnel). Early availability of personnel and data is essential, as 
their absence can affect critical RBPS elements such as workforce 
involvement, process knowledge management, management of change, 
hazards identification, risk analysis, and conduct of operations. De­
ficiencies in these areas can cascade into broader safety issues, 
compromising the long-term safe operation of the facility. While the case 
of asset acquisition without personnel transfer may be extreme, it is 
common and depicts an acute case of IKT failure, highlighting the risks 
of ageing asset transfers and the need for regulatory frameworks to help 
prevent major offshore accidents.
It should be noted that the findings are based on a single case study 
and on presenting summarized challenges to process safety experts and 
practitioners. As such, the findings are context-specific and not intended 
to be generalized across settings.
The research design is not free from cognitive biases; for instance, the 
“availability heuristic” may have influenced the selection of examples 
and, consequently, the responses. The way in which the challenges were 
presented is also naturally subject to the “framing effect”, thereby 
influencing how interviewees understood data and acted on it. Addi­
tionally, the use of snowball sampling, while effective for reaching 
experienced professionals, may have introduced sample bias by limiting 
the diversity of perspectives through reliance on professional networks. 
Although there was a worsening of incident indicators for Platform X, it 
is not possible to directly attribute this to unsuccessful IKT. Such asso­
ciations should be interpreted with caution, as it is difficult to disen­
tangle which impacts stem from knowledge transfer failures and which 
are consequences of asset ageing or differing organizational safety cul­
tures. Nonetheless, the change in incident rates before and after the 
transfer is notable and likely influenced by a combination of these 
factors.
Future studies could explore incident data from other units that have 
undergone unsuccessful IKT processes to better understand this rela­
tionship, as well as data from ageing facilities that did not undergo a 
knowledge transfer. In addition, comparative case studies involving 
different asset transitions or knowledge transfer models could help 
validate and expand upon the patterns observed in this research.
