Natural resources are a critical component of economic growth in many low and 
middle income countries. But resource dependence is often associated with mac-
roeconomic imbalances (Corden and┬á Neary 1982; Harding and┬á Venables 2016), 
vulnerability to commodity shocks (van┬áder Ploeg and┬áVenables 2012), and authori-
tarianism and poor governance (Ross 2015). One of the most robust and deleterious 
symptoms of the ┬şso-called ÔÇťresource curseÔÇŁ is civil conflict, a phenomenon partic-
ularly pronounced when commodity prices spike in oil and ┬şmineral-rich countries 
(Blair, Christensen, and┬áRudkin 2021). In weakly institutionalized states, resource 
wealth can engender violent competition over appropriable rents.

Even in the absence of ┬şfull-scale civil war, natural resource extraction may be 
plagued by black markets run by organized criminal and armed groups.1 These 
rackets of violence, theft, and extortion impose substantial costs on extractive 
firms (Blair, Christensen, and┬áWirtschafter 2022), who should be willing to pay 
the state for protection from predation. Instead, firms rely on private security yet 
assets remain vulnerable, while criminal groups are often implicitly supported by 
law enforcement corruption. These challenges can force multinational corporations 
(MNCs) to exit difficult markets entirely (Burger, Ianchovichina, and┬áRijkers 2016). 
In 2021, Shell announced that it would leave Nigeria after 65 years as the countryÔÇÖs 
leading oil producer, with plans to divest more than $2 billion in productive assets. 
This dramatic decision was a reaction to decades of steadily worsening losses from 
oil theft and widespread pipeline sabotage (Stakeholder Democracy NetworkÔÇôSDN 
2021).
Why does the state fail to protect these firms, forgoing essential resource rev-
enues? Such inefficient outcomes suggest a glaring failure of Coasean bargaining 
(Acemoglu 2003). One explanation is law enforcement corruptionÔÇöin a limited 
commitment environment, a lucrative illegal sector creates strong incentives to 
allow criminality.2 While large multinational firms have historically struggled to 
incentivize the state to protect their assets, indigenous firms may have a compara-
tive advantage in corruption (Javorcik and┬áWei 2009). By appointing political and 
military elites as shareholders and directors, local firms can make security agents 
residual claimants on private resource income. In return, local firms may obtain 
protection from criminal predation, allowing them to increase production, relative 
to ┬şbetter-resourced multinational peers.
I study the benefits of localness in the context of the Nigerian petroleum sector, 
an industry fraught with political violence, corruption, and ┬şblack-market oil theft. 
Following a major policy reform, the share of onshore output produced by local 
firms grew from just 6.4 percent in 2006 to 40 percent in 2016. Using detailed 
panel data on NigeriaÔÇÖs oil fields, I leverage this wave of divestment to study the 
role of local participation in oil field performance, resource theft, violence, and law 
enforcement corruption.
Whether divestment improves oil field performance and aggregate welfare is the-
oretically ambiguous. On the one hand, multinationals are likely to have techno-
logical and management advantages that boost their technical efficiency relative to 
local firms. At the same time, since criminal groups in the Nigerian oil sector oper-
ate under the protection of the state security forces (SDN 2019a), local firms may 
leverage superior political connections to secure their assets, outweighing technical 
disadvantages. To illustrate this dynamic, I develop a simple model in which a firm 
bargains with the state to obtain law enforcement protection, where government 
forces maintain the outside option of collecting criminal bribes and allowing theft. 
Importantly, theft is not just a transfer, but entails efficiency lossesÔÇöstolen output

In the ┬şsecond-best case, corruption allows local advantage to arise. Even if the

Commitment problems further underscore the importance of political connec-
tions. If the security forces can renege on any deal, ┬şlump-sum bribes are rendered 
ineffective. Only private claims to oil surplus can incentivize state agents to fight 
theft. Political connections are not just valuable, but are, indeed, a binding constraint 
to law enforcement provision. One source of local advantage, then, is the ability to 
appoint influential political and military elites as shareholders and board members 
in exchange for asset protection.
The key prediction of the model is that if local firms are more politically connected, 
then indigenization should result in more enforcement, less oil theft and violence, 
and more output captured by the legal market. I take this prediction to the data, study-
ing the evolution of oil field outcomes on divested assets relative to a control group. 
To identify the role of local take-over, I employ a ┬şdifference-in-differences approach 
that exploits changes in field ownership driven by multinational divestment. Using 
┬ştwo-way fixed effects (TWFE), I find that divested assets produce roughly 30 per-
cent more legal output. This ÔÇťlocal advantageÔÇŁ occurs despite local firms exhibiting 
lower quality. Divested fields experience 10 percent more oil spills due to mechani-
cal failure and flare 41 percent more gas annually, likely reflecting inferior operating 
standards, management, and maintenance of physical infrastructure.
As hypothesized, local firms see greater output despite lower quality by mitigat-
ing political risk: divestment leads to a sustained reduction in incidents of oil theft 
and violent conflict. On average, localized fields experience 5.7 fewer theft inci-
dents, a 54 percent decline, and 3 fewer conflict fatalities annually. In magnitudes, 
the reduction in oil theft can explain 66 percent of the output gain. Decomposing 
this 66 percent, I show that 24 percent of the output gain represents direct transfers 
from armed groups to firms and the state, while 42 percent represents recouped 
production that would otherwise have been deferred (i.e., kept in the ground) in the 
face of theft.

null effects. Security connections are additionally associated with greater output and 
more enforcement activity.
Taken together, the evidence suggests that local firms leverage networks of mil-
itary elites to align incentives between firms and the security forces and obtain law 
enforcement protection for their assets. These results explain the puzzle of why 
multinationals lack state protection in a ┬şsecond-best world even if they can deliver 
higher tax revenues in a ┬şfirst-best environmentÔÇöwhile a strong state might maxi-
mize oil sector profits, weak institutions allow the security forces to leverage their 
monopoly of violence for private gain.
I also find suggestive evidence in favor of additional mechanisms contained 
in the model. Among multinational assets, exposure to a foreign corruption law 
is associated with greater ┬şfield-level theft and violence, suggesting that the local 
advantage stems in part from the lower costs of corruption. In addition, I use 
data on oil licenses to show that indigenization increases the equity share of the 
operating firm by 11 percent. With larger ownership stakes, local operators inter-
nalize a greater share of theft losses, increasing willingness to pay for protection. 
I consider several alternative explanations for the resultsÔÇöcrime and employment 
spillovers, discount rates, ┬şhost-community investments, collusion with gangs, and 
local grievance toward multinationalsÔÇöand find no support for any of these alter-
native mechanisms.

Lastly, I evaluate whether the indigenization program raised government revenue 
and total welfare. Indigenization increases ┬şper-field government revenue by 40 per-
cent on average, generating $26.5 billion in additional revenue from ┬ş2006ÔÇô2016, or 
3.8 percent of sectoral output. The model delivers a formula highlighting the wel-
fare tradeoffs of indigenization. While divestment increases output, the surplus gain 
depends on ┬şlocal-MNC cost differentials. Declining oil theft recoups black market 
losses, including spillage, price discounts, and extraction costs, though at the cost 
of increased security expenditures. Finally, indigenization generates environmental 
externalities via rising oil production and gas flaring but also falling oil spills. In a 
┬şback-of-the-envelope exercise, I derive an upper bound of $22.9 billion in net gains, 
or 5.7 percent of NigeriaÔÇÖs 2016 GDP. Under more conservative assumptions about 
new output creation and black market price discounts, the estimates are 2.4 percent 
and 3.3 percent of GDP, respectively. Importantly, these welfare estimates take as 
given the institutional features of corruption and theft. Indigenization is, therefore, a 
┬şsecond-best policy response to an environment with weak institutions and insecure 
property rights, rather than a global optimum.
A ┬şlong-running literature in economics, stretching at least as far back as 
Hirschman (1969), debates the costs and benefits of multinational investment in 
developing economies. Multinationals are generally more productive, with better 
human capital, technology, and management practices (Aitken and┬áHarrison 1999; 
Arnold and Javorcik 2009; Guadalupe, Kuzmina, and┬áThomas 2012).3 Despite these 
advantages, ÔÇťinstitutional voidsÔÇŁ in developing countries may frustrate multinational 
operations (Palepu and┬áKhanna 1998) and deter investment (Burger, Ianchovichina, 
and Rijkers 2016; Blair, Christensen, and Wirtschafter 2022). Evidence suggests 
that corruption encourages joint ventures as multinationals seek to navigate local 
politics (Javorcik and┬áWei 2009), and that ┬şanti-bribery laws reduce multinational 
competitiveness (Chapman et┬áal. 2021).4 Nevertheless, direct comparisons of mul-
tinational and local operations in ┬şconflict-affected markets are limited. I show that 
in these ┬şsecond-best environments, indigenization may improve welfare, even after 
accounting for environmental and social externalities. This paper therefore illumi-
nates new and important tradeoffs in an old debate.

I.ÔÇé Background
A.ÔÇé The Nigerian Oil Sector
Nigeria is the largest ┬şoil producer in Africa. Rich deposits of crude oil are located 
onshore and in the waters of the Niger Delta, a region in southern Nigeria where 
the mouth of the Niger River meets the Gulf of Guinea. The Niger Delta consists of 
nine statesÔÇöAbia, Bayelsa, Delta, Rivers, Akwa Ibom, Imo, Ondo, Edo, and Cross 
RiverÔÇöand is home to 22 percent of NigeriaÔÇÖs population and numerous ethnic 
minority groups (NBS 2017). Since oil discovery in 1956, the sector has been domi-
nated by oil super majors Shell, Chevron, ExxonMobil, Total, and Eni. In 2004, these 
multinational companies produced 93.5 percent of NigeriaÔÇÖs 2.49 million barrels per 
day. In that year, the sector was valued at $45.8 billion in 2019 US dollars, or 98 per-
cent of NigeriaÔÇÖs export earnings. Multinationals operate ┬şprofit-sharing agreements 
with the ┬şstate-run oil company, the Nigerian National Petroleum Company (NNPC), 
structured as joint ventures, ┬şproduction-sharing contracts, or ┬şfee-for-service con-
tracts. Shares in new or expiring oil blocks are awarded by the state in a competitive 
bid process. This leads to substantial variation in the share of profits claimed by the 
operator of a given oil field (Supplemental Appendix Figure A3).
Throughout its postcolonial history, Nigeria has typified nearly all of the negative 
syndromes of the resource curse: authoritarian government, political instability, civil 
conflict, corruption, environmental devastation, high inequality, and poor and vola-
tile economic growth (Watts 2003; Caselli and┬áCunningham 2009). These ┬şoutcomes

resources to the shallow and increasingly deep-water reserves of the Gulf of Guinea. 
Offshore assets are costly to reach for oil thieves and militants, though they entail 
much larger exploration and production costs for firms. Between 2002 and 2016, 
the share of Nigerian oil produced from onshore fields fell by half, from 60 percent 
to just above 30 percent. As multinationals face oil theft losses, recurrent militancy, 
and ballooning private security costs (SDN 2021), they have often opted to divest

Here I describe the data used to test for local advantage. For greater detail on 
data sources, the construction of key variables, and measurement error issues, see

A.ÔÇé Oil Sector Data
Field Output and Location.ÔÇöAn annual panel of 314 active Nigerian oil fields 
forms the core of the data. ┬şField-level data on oil production come from the Annual 
Statistical Bulletin of the NNPC, augmented with data from the Department of 
Petroleum Resources (DPR), covering the years ┬ş1998ÔÇô2016. In each year, I record 
total output for the field, as well as the identity of the operating firm. A field enters 
the dataset in the year it first appears in these administrative records, and remains 
thereafter even if output is missing in a subsequent year, creating an unbalanced 
panel. ┬şTime-invariant ┬şfield-level covariates are the number of wells, date of com-
pletion of the first well, and well depth. I link fields to information on other out-
comes and geospatial control variables using centroid coordinates. Production data 
is reported to the Nigerian state by firms for the purposes of royalty calculation and 
so is net of losses, including theft, sabotage, and spillage.

III.ÔÇé Theoretical Framework
A.ÔÇé Model
What are the implications of multinational divestment for oil field performance 
and aggregate welfare? While local firms may have higher extraction costs than mul-
tinationals, they are likely to possess political connections that allow them to navi-
gate an environment characterized by weak property rights and state ┬şrent-seeking. 
In this section, I formalize this intuition in a model that demonstrates how cor-
ruption, insecurity, and commitment problems can combine to improve oil field 


## Page 14

1032

MARCH 2025
outcomes under divestment. I relegate the details to Supplemental Appendix B, and 
here describe the strategic environment and provide intuition for key predictions.
Firms bargain with state security agents over protection from oil theft. The stage 
game is a ┬şsequential-move interaction between firms, oil theft gangs, and a state 
security agent. The firm produces a fixed level of oil output, sold at the international 
oil price. The firm and the gang both offer bribes to the state. If law enforcement 
accepts the gangÔÇÖs bribe, there is no enforcement and the gang steals a constant quan-
tity at a fixed cost. Theft is inefficient because (i) gangs incur extraction costs, (ii) it 
directly destroys output, and (iii) stolen oil is sold on the black market at a discount. 
If the state instead accepts the firmÔÇÖs bribe, then the state enforces the law at a fixed 
cost, reducing the expected level of theft.12 Firms have two typesÔÇömultinational 
and localÔÇöand several sources of heterogeneity. If the firmÔÇÖs bribe is accepted, the 
firm pays a penalty capturing the legal costs of corruption. Law enforcement may 
internalize the firmÔÇÖs profits, measuring the strength of political connections. Firms 
also differ across fixed extraction costs.
I consider several cases of the equilibrium, progressively adding elements to 
build intuition. In the ┬şfirst-best case, there is no corruption or oil theft, and law 
enforcement is unnecessary. When allowing for theft, the law is enforced when the 
value of reclaimed oil exceed the costs of enforcement. Whenever enforcement is 
socially valuable, it is also privately optimal to the state, and so the stateÔÇÖs incentives 
are aligned with welfare maximization. Despite this, there is leakage into the black 
market since enforcement is imperfect, so both legal output and total welfare fall 
relative to the ┬şfirst-best. Still, equilibrium welfare is maximized by the ┬şlowest-cost 
producer, since firm heterogeneity enters only through costs. Thus, if local firms are 
less efficient than multinationals, there is no socially productive role for local firms.
The ┬şsecond-best case introduces bribes and political connections. Still, the state 
must perfectly commit to protecting the party with whom it contracts. In equilibrium, 
government enforcement is higher for more connected firms because it lowers the 
threshold bribe required to sway the state toward enforcement. Enforcement also 
rises in the firmÔÇÖs ownership share, and falls in corruption cost, by increasing and 
decreasing firm ┬şwillingness-to-pay, respectively. Enforcement declines in policing 
cost and increases in policing effectiveness. Bargaining frictions undermine Coasean 
transfers, so theft may occur in equilibrium despite its inefficiency. However, when 
firms are politically connected, government more fully internalizes the losses from 
theft, lowering the cost of bribery and increasing the scope for cooperation. Legal 
output and social surplus both fall relative to ┬şfirst-best and ┬şno-corruption cases, since 
enforcement is both imperfect and discretionary. Since enforcement and therefore 
theft losses depend on firm identity, the ┬şlowest-cost firm no longer necessarily maxi-
mizes surplus. Instead, if local firms are better connected, they will enjoy preferential 
enforcement, less theft, and more legal output, all else equal. This is the ÔÇťlocal advan-
tageÔÇŁ and constitutes the main empirical prediction of the model.
The assumption that all parties can perfectly commit may be optimistic. In real-
ity, law enforcement might accept bribes from firms and allow theft nonetheless, 
given the cost of enforcement and the lure of ┬şblack-market rents. The final case of

Ownership Consolidation: Operators with larger ownership stakes internalize a 
greater share of the losses from theft. In Supplemental Appendix F.F2, I use licensing 
data to show that divestment increases the field ownership Herfindahl index by 6.6 
percent, and increases the ownership stake of the operating firm by 10.7 percent 
(Table A30). There is no change in the NNPC stake, suggesting consolidation of 
MNC stakes during divestment drives local firmsÔÇÖ greater equity interest.
Corruption Penalties: Multinational firms may face higher expected costs of 
engaging in corrupt behavior because of ┬şhome-country ┬şanticorruption statutes, 
such as the FCPA in the United States. Given the relatively broad definitions of 
foreign officials contained in these laws, the prospect of legal liabilities could 
plausibly deter multinationals from bargaining with political ┬şpower brokers. In 
Supplemental Appendix F.F3, I test for a relationship between international cor-
ruption law and field performance. I find that passage of a ┬şhome-country corruption 
law is associated with a statistically significant annual increase in theft and violent

small sample (there are only five distinct home countries), the evidence suggests 
that ┬şanti-corruption laws affect multinationalsÔÇÖ ability to use corrupt payments to

D.ÔÇé Alternative Explanations
Spatial Spillovers: In general equilibrium, divestment could increase targeting 
of multinational fields if local fields are politically protected but their multinational 
neighbors are not. Such spillovers may bias the treatment effect (Rubin 2005), and 
would have important implications for aggregate welfare. In Supplemental Appendix 
G.G1, I estimate negligible spillovers in theft or output (Figure A18); accounting for 
spillovers does not substantially alter the main estimates.
Dynamic Extraction: Local firms may have different optimal extraction profiles 
for many reasonsÔÇöthe opportunity cost of capital, market power in local refining, 
or unpriced externalities. Any change in output following divestment might simply 
reflect a new optimal dynamic production path. Without a fully dynamic model of 
exhaustible resource extraction, the relationship of these differing extraction profiles 
to the social optimum is unclear However, as Anderson, Kellogg, and Salant (2018) 
show, oil production at the ┬şwell-level does not typically following Hotelling (1931) 
dynamics, because it is constrained by reservoir pressure and therefore difficult to 
adjust along the intensive margin in the ┬şshort-run. Instead, firms adjust by drilling 
new wells and so ┬şfield-level changes in optimal extraction are likely to be observed 
only over the ┬şlonger-run. Given that our data run only a decade, substantial new 
drilling and production is unlikely. In fact, ┬şwell-drilling data indicate that just 1.75 
percent of the total stock of wells was drilled during the sample period. Further, most 
of the increase in output from localization can be attributed to reducing theft losses 
(Supplemental Appendix C.C3). In Supplemental Appendix G.G2, I estimate non-
parametric ┬şage-extraction profiles for multinational and local fields (Figure A19); 
the extraction profiles appear similar, consistent with minimal dynamic adjustment 
by local firms.

by grievance rather than economic motives (Buhaug, Cederman, and Gleditsch 
2014). Local sentiments toward Nigerian companies may be considerably better 
than toward multinationals. If so, there might be a reduction in community pro-

as the outcome variables (Table A32). There is no evidence of a change in grievance 
after localization.
Local Economic Spillovers: Divestment may generate positive economic spill-
overs, bidding up the opportunity cost of joining gangs and reducing crime. To test 
this hypothesis, I use a national panel survey of ┬şworking-age Nigerians, linking 
each household to its nearest oil field. In Supplemental Appendix G.G4, I find no 
evidence of employment or consumption spillovers from local ownership (Table 
A33). This finding stands in contrast to studies on local resource booms, which 
find positive spillovers by bidding up demand for local labor and inputs (Allcott 
and┬áKeniston 2017) or driving local fiscal expansion (Caselli and┬áMichaels 2013). 
This discrepancy is due to the fact that divestment primarily recoups lost output 
on existing assets, rather than representing an expansion on the extensive margin. 
Furthermore, the lack of fiscal response is to be expected; revenues from the oil sec-
tor are centrally collected and then allocated to state and local governments using 
a ┬şpredetermined formula (Fetzer and┬áKyburz 2022), which depends primarily on 
fixed characteristics such as population and landmass. Therefore, excess oil revenue 
induced by indigenization should not affect the fiscal position of local governments 
in which divestment occurs.
Targeted CSR Investment: The most visible local benefits of oil extraction are 
typically not jobs but rather host community investments in the form of corporate 
social responsibility (CSR). Oil companies may prefer to provide CSR benefits to 
troubled areas than to negotiate protection directly. If local firms are better corporate 
citizens, this could account for localization benefits.19 In Supplemental Appendix 
G.G5, I use ┬şcross-sectional data on oil company CSR projects in 2016 to test whether 
local firms are better at targeting their investments. Though these ┬şcross-sectional cor-
relations are somewhat speculative, I find that, if anything, multinational CSR invest-
ments are more responsive to recent levels of local militant violence (Figure A20).

parallel trends, but there is no evidence for this in Figure┬á3. Second, this explana-
tion is not consistent with a rise in enforcement after divestment (Table┬á4), since

VIII.ÔÇé Conclusion
Firms in the natural resource sectors of weak states are subject to violent extortion 
by armed groups and suffer losses to illicit markets. Despite the fact that this creates a 
willingness to pay for protection, the state typically fails to protect these firms. I study 
the roots of this bargaining failure in the context of the Nigerian oil sector. I argue that 
in NigeriaÔÇÖs oil sector, and others like it, where militant groups and organized crime 
are threats to firm operations and corruption buys law enforcement protection, local 
companies possess distinct advantages over their ┬şbetter-resourced multinational peers.
Using data on Nigerian oil fields from ┬ş2006ÔÇô2016, I find that oil output captured 
by the legal market rises after divestment to local firms by nearly 1 million barrels per 
year, a 33 percent gain. However, consistent with an operational disadvantage, local 
fields experience more oil spills from equipment malfunctions and flare more natural 
gas. The key to the local output advantage lies in the ┬şmultibillion dollar black market 
for stolen oil. I find that local assets experience 54 percent less oil theft. I further find 
that these gains are concentrated in the onshore fields most susceptible to crime and 
violence, whereas the technical oil spill losses are concentrated on offshore fields 
with high technological requirements. This further underscores that while multina-
tionals have a technology advantage, the black market generates a much larger local 
advantage. Placebo tests using data on the universe of Nigerian oil and gas corporate 
transactions rule out spurious transition effects and unobserved differential trends.
A model of the bargaining interaction between firms and law enforcement shows 
that better political connections allow local firms to expand the scope for dealmak-
ing, purchase law enforcement protection, and increase output captured in the legal 
market. In the ┬şno-commitment case, political connections are a binding constraint to 
law enforcement provision, and unconnected firms are left to the mercy of rapacious 
armed groups.
To test whether connections drive local advantage via law enforcement corrup-
tion, I first show that local firms indeed receive preferential state protection: local-
ization leads to an 83 percent increase in law enforcement actions against oil theft. 
Using data on firmsÔÇÖ boards and shareholders, I show that political connections

The welfare estimates imply that the indigenization program generated gains worth

by dirtier local firms. Although productivity differences are unmeasured, these welfare

enough to outweigh the loss of multinational productivity, and therefore justify indig-

tradeoffs in allowing politically connected local firms to dominate the marketplace, 
raising questions about the incidence of gains. The findings also raise important ques-
tions around why multinationals fail to identify and build effective political influence 
networks, for example by using joint ventures. I leave these for future work.
Lastly, I argue that the Nigerian oil and gas sector is closer to a representative 
than a pathological case. In extractive industries across the globeÔÇöfrom Congolese 
minerals to Colombian goldÔÇöfirms face a complex political economy characterized 
by black markets, organized crime, armed groups, and corrupt politicians. In these 
┬şsecond-best cases, we must reconsider the role of indigenous firms and the benefits 
of foreign investment.
