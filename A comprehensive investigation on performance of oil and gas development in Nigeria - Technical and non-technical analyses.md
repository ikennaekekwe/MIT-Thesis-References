Abstract
Nigeria is a critical player in the energy industry. However, the industry has concerns over the 
underperformance of Nigerian oil and gas projects. This study involved comprehensive and quantitative 
analysis of Nigerian oil and gas project performance from technical and non-technical perspectives with 
data from 65 projects. Nigerian projects have a high average cost overrun of 38% with an SD of 39%, 
time overrun of 37% with an SD of 41%, fatality rate of 0.027, and oil spillage ratio of 18.51. The overall 
performance of Nigerian projects is much worse than that for their global peers. The results demonstrate 
that Nigerian oil and gas projects are of low subsurface complexity and present low technology 
challenges, which are advantages for field development; therefore, subsurface complexity and technology 
challenges are not the major causes of the poor performance. However, non-technical factors (local 
content, community, security, and partnership) play a significant role. In the study, the degree of the 
factor’s effect is quantified and categorized into groups for guidance. Further, a set of recommended 
practices was developed based on the risk level to assist project planners, international companies, 
investors, and policymakers to mitigate risks of developing oil and gas projects.
Keywords: Project performance, Cost and schedule, Safety and environment, Subsurface characteristics, 
Nigerian oil and gas, Technical and non-technical analyses

Introduction
Nigeria is a key player in the Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries and world energy 
industry. There are about 37 billion barrels (bbl.) of estimated proved oil reserves in Nigeria, which is 
ranked No. 10 in the world, and 192 trillion cubic feet (tcf) of natural gas reserves, ranked 7th in the 
world [1]. Also, the oil industry in Nigeria plays a significant role in the Nigerian economy. The daily oil 
production is about 2.43 million bbl. and the annual gas production is 1.35 tcf. Revenue from the oil and 
gas sector, therefore, accounts for over 90% of exports and more 80% of government income [1]. In 
general, with an abundance of resources, the Nigerian oil and gas sectors exert considerable influence 
domestically and internationally. 
Nigeria has a prolific and significant hydrocarbon deposited delta system that is larger than 300,000 
km2 with a sediment thickness of up to 10 km (Figure 1). In addition to the large volume of resources, the 
crude oil and natural gas produced are of excellent physical quality. Most of the Nigerian crude oil is light 
crude oil with a gravity between 21 ºAPI and 45 ºAPI with a low sulfur content [2]. The natural gas also 
is of high quality with rich liquid and a low sulfur percentage. However, about 75% of the produced 
natural gas is re-injected into the well or flared instead of transported to clients to realize economic value 
due to the lack of facilities and infrastructure [3]. In general, Nigeria has a vast and rich oil and gas 
resource with excellent quality based on the resource distribution and oil and gas properties. Therefore, 
Nigeria is always an attractive location for new oil and gas project development. More than $13 billion in 
annual capital investment is spent in the upstream sector for developing oil and gas projects in Nigeria [4]. 
However, the recent oil price crash has made all international oil companies and national oil companies 
cut capital spending significantly by about 40% [4]. Many planned or ongoing projects were canceled or 
delayed with capital constraints. The attention on or concern over the Nigerian oil and gas projects’ 
underperformance has risen from various groups, including oil and gas companies, governments, and 
contractors. The Nigerian projects tend to perform poorly with respect to cost and schedule. The high cost 
overruns and schedule delays have become an obstacle to motivate oil and gas companies to invest 
because of significant uncertainty. Investors also acknowledge that the risk from cost overruns of large 
individual projects sometimes can lead to bankruptcy or financial difficulties, as the average capital cost 
of large oil and gas projects is more than $2 billion. The Nigerian oil and gas industry has started to think 
about how to make oil and gas projects more competitive and more attractive at a low oil price and in a 
low margin industry. How to quantitively assess oil and gas project performance and improve this 
performance have become hot topics in the international oil and gas sector. This study will be the first 
comprehensive study to investigate Nigerian oil and gas project performance with actual project data and 
provides great insight for improving oil and gas project performance for operators and the Nigerian 
government.

 Literature Review
A comprehensive literature review was conducted in this section to understand the current project 
performance. Defining project performance is the first step to measuring or evaluating oil and gas project 
performance. Through a literature review, we found that the measurement of project performance varies 
and depends on the type of project, project stage, and the project’s structural level. Many key 
performance indicators were proposed and applied to assess project performance. The U.S. Department of 
Energy (U.S. DOE) proposed different performance indicators for measuring the performance at the 
program and project levels [5]. Project-level measurement is defined as assessing the outcomes of an 
individual project with a given resource. The program-level measurement is used to assess the outcomes 
of all projects within a program with a given total resource for the whole program. The U.S. DOE 
proposed 10 categories of key performance indicators to evaluate project-level performance, including 
cost growth, schedule slip, phase cost factors, phase schedule factors, preliminary and design engineering 
factors, cost variance, schedule variance, cost performance index, schedule performance, safety 
performance index, and safety performance measures [5]. Hammadi and Bernard listed five key 
performance indicators: cost, time, quality, HSE, and satisfaction for regular construction projects [6]. Eti 
et al. developed a maintenance measurement indicator for Nigerian maintenance projects [7]. Eti et al. 
developed a productivity index to assess the petrochemical industry in Nigeria between 1997 and 2001 [8]. 
Sun et al. investigated energy conservation project performance based on investigations of their

Overview of Oil and Gas Project Scope and Development Process
Understanding oil and gas project scopes and the project life cycle is the basis for evaluating oil 
and gas project performance. Oil and gas projects are developed to extract oil and gas from the subsurface 
and process crude oil and gas and then transport them to a refinery plant or customer through various 
transportation methods. Figure 2 shows a typical offshore project hierarchical structure. The typical 
integrated oil and gas project scope includes the well, production and processing facilities and pipeline 
subscopes and each project subscope includes its various subcomponents. As shown in Figure 3, an 
offshore FPSO project profile includes an FPSO ship, subsea production system, offshore well, various 
types of pipelines, riser, and umbilical [66].

Recognizing the oil and gas project development phase is also extremely important for evaluating 
oil and gas project performance and identifying the drivers of project performance. The typical oil and gas 
project life cycle, as shown in Figure 4, includes six major steps: discovery, evaluation, development, 
production, and abandonment. Discovery step: the discovery step is the first step to determine the location 
of the hydrocarbon accumulation. If the petroleum is stored in a reservoir hundreds or thousands meters 
away from the surface, surface seismic exploration using various tools or equipment is conducted to 
locate the reservoir, and then exploration wells are drilled to confirm whether the prospect has 
hydrocarbons and the detailed depth and location of the hydrocarbon accumulation. Evaluation step: with 
basic information from the discovery step, the appraisal well is drilled to collect more data through the 
various samples and tests to describe the reservoir and field. Meanwhile, different reservoir simulations or 
evaluations are implemented to understand the properties of the reservoir, fluid, rock, etc., and determine 
the hydrocarbon reserve for development. The cost of the discovery phase and evaluation phase accounts 
for about 10 to 20% of the total cost. Development phases: the development phase is the most important 
of the pre-operation phases. The first part of the development phase, well types, types of production 
facilities, transportation routine, etc., will be confirmed and all basis of design specifications will be 
finished, which is also called the detailed engineering phase. The second part of the development phase is 
the execution phase: various project scopes are built or constructed in execution according to the 
development plan and execution plan. The most capital cost will be spent in the execution phase,

accounting for 20% to 50% of the whole project cycle cost. Production stage: the hydrocarbons will be 
produced through developed well and production facilities and transported to the customer or clients to 
generate revenue for oil and gas operators. The production period can last about 15 to 30 years; a giant 
field may continue to produce for more than 50 years. As shown in Figure 5, the production profile has a

production phase cost, mostly operations costs, accounts for about 50% to 60% of the total project cycle

phase, the hydrocarbon production declines to a certain volume that is insufficient to support economical 
production, so the reservoir and field have to be abandoned. The facilities will be dismantled, the well 
will be abandoned and plugged, and the site will be cleaned and restored. Understanding the oil and gas 
project scope and various project development phases will be helpful for gaining an understanding of oil 
and gas project performance.

Data and Methodology 
In this study, sufficient and good quality project information provides a solid basis and 
foundation for investigating Nigerian oil and gas project performance and quantifying the effect of the 
causes or drivers of this performance. Based on a literature review, various methods or approaches were 
used to attain project data. Some scholars designed a survey and distributed it to various companies to 
collect project data [52, 59]. Some researchers cooperated with large international agencies or companies 
and unitized their database to investigate their project performance. Ahsan analyzed the data from 100 
projects with support from Asian development banks [64]. Pohl and Dubaravko studied the data from 
1,000 projects from the World Bank [45]. Other researchers used public data to investigate project 
performance, such as a public database, company annual report, etc. Rui et al. analyzed 220 pipeline 
projects and 430 onshore compressor projects from the annual O&G journal databook [33, 34, 37]. 
The data in this study were collected from various public sources and a survey of various experts. 
By reviewing available data sources and literature in the public domain, information on 65 projects was 
collected. The major data sources included the Business Monitor International, Society of Petroleum 
Engineers, the Offshore Technology website, the Offshore Quest website, The Petroleum Regulatory 
Agency of Nigeria (PRAN), the Nigerian National Petroleum Corporation (NNPC), Oil & Gas Journal, 
Subseiq website, analyst reports from various financial institutions, an oil and gas company’s annual 
report, individual expert inquires through the Society of Petroleum Engineers network, etc. The most 
available data for each project were prepared with the best effort. Information from a total of 65 projects 
was collected for this study. The project sizes vary from $62 million to $6 billion with an average cost of 
$900 million. The start year for construction for these projects ranges from 2003 to 2014. To recognize 
the data pattern and differences among the two groups, a T-test, a statistical technique, was used to verify 
if there was a significant difference between the two groups based on the p-value number generated. In 
statistics, a p-value smaller than 5% is typically considered significant.

Key Performance Indicator for Evaluating Nigeria Oil and Gas Projects
According to the literature review, various performance metrics or indicators were generated to 
assess project performance. With the data availability and importance of performance indicators taken 
into consideration, this study will determine Nigeria project oil and gas performance regarding cost, 
schedule, and safety. Cost performance always is an essential indicator for assessing project outcomes. 
Under the current low oil price scenario, the budget for oil and gas project investment is tight for all oil 
and gas companies. As such, cost performance is even more critical for new oil and gas projects. The 
capital cost overrun of oil and gas projects was selected as a project cost performance indicator for 
evaluating cost performance in this study. Schedule performance is still an essential indicator for oil and 
gas projects. The schedule is always considered significant for oil and gas project planning and the 
execution phase. The schedule delay will affect the subsequent phases and increase the cost and delay oil 
production. Although there is always a trade-off between cost and schedule, oil and gas companies still 
want to deliver the project as fast as possible to produce oil and attain revenue under a specific budget. 
The time overrun indicator was selected to assess project schedule performance. The time overrun was 
generated using the planned project development duration and actual development duration. The 
production index is also considered to be an important indicator to evaluate oil and gas project quality. 
However, the production performance data are always hard to collect and seldom reported. Therefore, a 
production performance evaluation is not included in this study. In addition to cost and schedule, safety 
performance becomes more vital. The incident rate for the oil and gas industry is significantly higher than 
for other industries because the work environment and product properties pose more safety risk to the 
employee [14]. Further, the safety performance of offshore oil and gas projects is much worse than that of 
onshore projects. The accident rate for offshore projects is two times that of onshore projects [68]. Also, 
the consequences of oil and gas projects are enormous. For example, the British Petroleum (BP) 
deepwater drilling rig explosion in the Gulf of Mexico killed 11 and injured 16 workers, discharged 5 
million barrels of oil, and cost BP more than $60 billion and caused considerable damage to the reputation 
for the whole industry [69]. Safety performance has become a more and more important indicator for 
determining the success of oil and gas projects. Therefore, safety performance was selected as one of the 
crucial indicators for evaluating Nigerian oil and gas projects. 
In summary, with the data availability and importance of the performance indicators taken into 
consideration, the cost performance, schedule performance, and safety performance were selected as the 
key indicators for assessing Nigerian oil and gas projects.

Overall Performance of Nigeria Projects
The overall cost and schedule performance of Nigeria projects is shown in Table 1. The average cost 
overrun is 38% with a range from -12% to 130%. An average cost overrun of 38% indicates that the 
actual project cost is 38% higher than the estimated cost, which is a significantly high cost overrun. To 
illustrate the scale of an average cost overrun of 38%, based on an average estimated project cost of $900 
million, the actual project spending would reach almost $1.4 billion. This level of cost overrun results in 
significant capital pressure and economic difficulty for companies or investors, especially in the current 
low oil price period. Also, the cost performance deviates by an average of 39%, implying that cost 
predictability is out of control for the Nigerian oil and gas industry as a whole. The project with the worst 
cost performance overran its estimate by more than 130%; the project with the best cost performance only 
saved 12% in cost. Compared to the reported average cost overrun for global oil and gas projects of 18%, 
the cost performance of Nigerian oil and gas projects is far worse than that in other countries or regions. 
As mentioned previously, an integrated oil and gas project often includes facilities, a well, and a pipeline. 
The cost performance for the major subscopes is also summarized in Table 1. The facilities have the 
highest average cost overrun of 42% with the most substantial standard deviation of 55%, followed by the 
well scope cost overrun of 36% with a standard deviation of 35% and the pipeline scope cost overrun of 
20% with a standard deviation of 13%. The cost performance of the different subscopes indicates that the 
cost performance varies across subscopes. The facilities scope, the largest subscope of oil and gas projects, 
has the highest overspending and largest deviation; the pipeline scope has a relatively better cost 
performance, but still averages a 20% cost overrun. In summary, Nigerian oil and gas project cost 
performance demonstrates that Nigerian oil and gas projects have a higher cost overrun and worse cost 
performance than their global peers, and cost overruns for the project subscopes vary.

7.1 Subsurface Complexity
Oil and gas projects have a unique feature compared to projects in other industries: extracting 
hydrocarbons from a reservoir hundreds of thousands of meters below the surface. Therefore, the 
subsurface characteristics are the first factor that should be investigated for assessing oil and gas project 
performance. The subsurface features determine the amount of hydrocarbons stored: the difficulty of 
extracting hydrocarbons from the underground, the selection of the technology to be employed, 
engineering designs for the well and production facilities, etc. For example, a high pressure and high 
temperature (HPHT) reservoir (reservoir temperature ≥300 ºF and reservoir pressure ≥10,000 psi) will 
affect the electronic function, physical strength, sealing technology, and chemical reaction of the process, 
which are beyond most of the current technology’s capacity [77]; hence, the new technology will have to 
be developed and implemented to recover oil from this HPHT reservoir. Therefore, understanding the 
subsurface characteristics of the Nigeria delta hydrocarbon system is the foundation for successfully 
developing and assessing oil and gas projects in Nigeria. 
The Nigeria delta system is a self-contained hydrocarbon system with three levels: the Oligocene, 
lower Miocene, and Eocene. The onshore section of the Nigeria delta was categorized into different 
depositional systems: Great Ughelli Depo-belt, Coastal Swamp Depo-belt, Northern Depo-belt, Central 
Swamp Depo-belt, and shallow offshore depobelts. Sediments contained in the onshore section become 
progressively younger toward the sea. The offshore section was divided into the Fold Belt, Inner Thrust 
Belt, and Outer Thrust Belt and is in a compressional regime. The Akata, Agbada, and Benin formations 
are the major stratigraphic for the Nigeria delta system. The plays in the shallow water were formed with 
a regular delta shoreface and shallow reservoirs. Understanding the distribution of the play and targeted 


## Page 17

ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT
16
play in each structure belt is critical for successful hydrocarbon exploration and production. In general, 
the primary targeted play in the northern delta deobelt and coastal swamp deobelt is the middle Eocene, 
and the middle Miocene play is the most prolific play in the Nigeria Delta. More than 300 exploration 
wells have been drilled so far to locate the Middle Miocene as the target play with a high success rate of 
about 70% of finding hydrocarbons [2]. The Middle Miocene is the primary target play and very 
productive and next to a narrow shelf area, which provides an excellent advantage for field development. 
The Nigeria delta system’s reservoir age ranges from Pliocene to Eocene. Most hydrocarbons are 
produced from unconsolidated sands and the sandstone of the Agbada formation for the shallow water 
and onshore areas. The thickness of the individual sands ranges from 15 m to 45 m. Because the 
sediments in the onshore or shallow water section are quite young, the reservoir quality is mostly 
determined by the types of the sand body instead of the reservoir depth. There are very high 
permeabilities up to serval darcies and a high porosity of 25% to 35%. In the deepwater delta section, the 
reservoir is more sporadic due to the sands deposited and retained on the top. However, the reservoir 
quality still ranges from good to very good. The porosity is often higher than 20%, and the hydrocarbon 
saturations range between 70% and 90%. The well success rate is about 70%, which is higher than the 
industry average. The reservoir characteristics summary and comparison are shown in Table 2. Nigerian 
oil and gas reservoir characteristics show an excellent advantage for field development compared to the 
global average for all major reservoir characteristics, including the porosity, permeability, hydrocarbon 
saturation, and fluid properties. The Nigeria delta systems have a much higher porosity, permeability, and 
hydrocarbon saturation; thicker reservoir formation; better crude oil quality; and highly successful drilling 
well rate. However, there were still a few unsuccessful drilling wells, but they are not caused by the 
subsurface complexity; instead, they are mostly due to the poor project practices used, including the lack 
of trap validation and definition, fault seal failure, the well being positioned away from the structure, the 
lack of structural culmination regarding the depth, and targeted zone failure. Based on the analysis of the 
subsurface characteristics, it can be concluded that the subsurface complexity of the Nigeria Delta system 
is low and presents a significant advantage for development. In addition, 100% of the projects in the study 
samples reported that there no or little subsurface challenges were faced during field development, 
indicating that the low subsurface complexity and homogenous reservoir with a large reserve is the great 
advantage that attracts operators to invest in Nigeria. Considering the results of an analysis of the physical 
subsurface characteristics of the Nigeria Delta system and information from projects in the study samples, 
it was concluded that the subsurface complexity of Nigerian oil and gas fields is much lower than industry 
average and poses a great advantage for development, and the subsurface complexity is not a significant 
driver of the worse project performance in Nigeria.

7.2 New Technology Issue
In addition to considering the subsurface complexity factor, another potential technical driver 
affecting project performance is the degree of new technology applied in Nigerian projects. From a global 
perspective, the development and deployment of new technology for oil and gas projects become more 
and more common for unlocking hydrocarbons from the more challenging reservoir over time. Various 
new technologies were used in different oil and gas projects in different regions, such as a subsea 
compressor, flexible pipeline, multi-branch drilling, smart completion, etc. The purpose of using a new 
technology in oil and gas projects is to reduce the project’s capital and operations costs, increase 
production, and enhance the competitiveness of oil and gas projects. An excellent example of a successful 
new technology application in the oil and gas industry is the hydraulic fracturing technology. The massive 
use of hydraulic fracturing technology makes producing unconventional resources of shale gas and oil 
feasible and efficient. However, the economic success of implementing the hydraulic fracture technology 
is achieved through an extended period of applications and practices. As shown in Figure 10, the 
breakeven price of shale oil from five major basins in the U.S. has decreased from an average of $72/bbl 
in 2013 to $48/bbl in 2016. Efficient well drilling and completion and increasing the well performance

significantly high and the unit costs tend to decrease by applying better practices and learnings as well as 
increasing the economic scale over time. Therefore, many oil and gas projects have a high cost overrun 
and face schedule delays because they are unable to identify appropriate new technology and predict the

indicates that low technology challenges significantly reduce the difficulty of the technical work. 
According to the analysis, it was concluded that applying new technology or significant technology 
challenges are uncommon for Nigerian oil and gas development. Because these low technology 
challenges in developing Nigeria oil and gas fields are an advantage for development, technology issue is

7.3 Local Content
Local content is considered a non-technical factor that potentially influences Nigerian project 
performance. Law always defines the local content within each country. Local content laws were 
implemented in 2006 in the Nigerian oil and gas industry to develop local competencies and capacities. 
The definition of local content varies by different scholar and by region. The Nigerian Oil and Gas 
Industry Content Development Act defines it as “the quantum of composite value added to or created in 
the Nigerian economy by a systematic development of capacity and capacities through the deliberate 
utilization of Nigerian human, material resources, and services in the Nigerian oil and gas industry” [86]. 
Before 2016, the Nigerian government found that there was only a small amount of oil and gas investment 
spent in Nigerian domestic projects and most investment involved outsourcing or foreign companies. To 
promote more oil and gas work and contract work to be handled by local Nigerian businesses and 
individuals, the Nigeria local content law was implemented in 2006, which required a certain percentage 
of the work to be done in Nigeria. The local content requirement increased from 45% in 2006 to 70% in 
2010, which is significant. Local content has become a more critical problem for oil and gas companies 
and the petroleum industry in Nigeria [87]. Many reports indicated that oil and gas project failures were 
caused by local content issues, including a high percentage of imported raw materials, lack of qualified 
professionals and labor with the technical skill competency for the assignment, inadequate infrastructure 
and power, etc. [86].

projects. Nigeria has one of the largest populations in the world, but the poor human development leads to 
a shortage of skilled labor. Based on the local content requirement, the project team has to use a local

undertake the assignment. For example, to complete platform fabrication with a local fabrication yard, the

fabrication; this involves extra time and costs. What is worse, often there was no qualified local labor to

7.4 Security Issues
Security has become a big concern for the oil and gas industry in Nigeria compared to other 
regions and countries. Security is defined as the protection from loss caused by illegal activities, including, 
but not limited to, fraud, fire, kidnapping, sabotage, theft, etc. The security factor includes asset security 
and individual security [89]. All oil and gas projects in this study faced different degrees of security 
challenges in production and transportation operation as well as safety challenges for their employees, 
especially for the expats. For some regions, the expats of international oil and gas companies are only 
present at the important events; the remaining time they stay at the base or home as much as possible to 
avoid unnecessary risk. Oil and gas companies have to hire a local contractor or security agent and set up 
a checkpoint to protect their asset and employees, which adds costs and time to execute projects. The 
security issue cost mostly includes hiring a security contractor or paying for a state or government force;

as a major factor to degrade Nigerian oil and gas project performance and cause huge revenue losses for

7.5 Community Issues
The local community is always considered an important stakeholder in oil and gas project 
development. Without support from the local community, the project is not able to proceed or be 
successfully implemented. Community issues are a common and important issue for the oil and gas 
industry. About 20% of projects in this study indicate that they had community issues. The data also show 
that community issues are more common for the onshore projects than offshore projects. The projects 
with community issues also had higher cost overruns and longer schedule delays, as indicated in Figure

Nigerian project performance (Pr < 0.05). Many scholars also suggested various reasons to explain the 
impact of community issues on oil and gas project performance without quantifying the impact of the 
community issues. Davis found that oil and gas companies spent substantial unexpected costs due to 
community disruptions and also that frequent temporary shutdowns or delays cause low productivity in 
execution and rising costs [91]. With further research, the environmental issues, insufficient project 
assistance, and insufficient government reaction to the community are the most common factors leading 
to community issues. Environmental issues are also the first to cause issues between the community and 
oil and gas projects, like a gas flare. Pollution, acid rain, and noise have deteriorated the farmland and 
rivers. Meanwhile, the compensation for environmental issues is inadequate to cover the losses as there is 
a big gap between the local community’s request and what oil and gas companies actually provide. 
Therefore, insufficient project assistance is listed as the second most common cause of community issues. 
The local community sometimes uses violent approaches to force companies to provide more facilities 
and assistance to the communities, leading to project shutdowns and facility damage and a decrease in

7.6 Partnership Format

project development. The joint venture partners of oil and gas projects can be a government agency, 
international oil company, national oil company, and/or financial institution. Joint ventures are even more 
popular in Nigeria. The joint venture partnership was adopted by almost all Nigerian oil and gas projects.

Conclusions
This study is composed of three major parts: first, a comprehensive analysis of overall Nigerian 
oil and gas project performance regarding the cost, schedule, and safety was conducted; second, the 


## Page 30

ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT
29
causes driving Nigerian oil and gas project performance was identified and the effect of the drivers was 
quantified; and third, recommendations for Best Practice in each critical area to improve Nigerian oil and 
gas project performance were developed. 
This study found that cost overruns and schedule delays are widespread across the different 
industries and regions. Compared to other industries, research on oil and gas project performance is 
insufficient, and research regarding Nigerian oil and gas project performance almost does not exist in the 
public domain. This study investigated Nigerian oil and gas project performance using sustainable project 
data. The overall performance analysis findings for the Nigerian oil and gas projects include: 1: The 
Nigerian projects have a total cost overrun of 38% with a standard deviation of 39% and the overall cost 
and schedule performance for Nigerian projects is much worse than that for global oil and gas projects; 2: 
The cost performance for the primary subscopes of Nigerian projects vary. The facilities scope has the 
highest cost overrun of 42%, the wells scope has a cost overrun of 36%, and the pipeline scope overruns 
its cost by 20%. 3: Nigeria projects slip their schedules by 37% with a standard deviation of 41%. 4: The 
safety of the Nigerian oil and gas industry is much worse than that for its global counterparts. There are 
more fatal incidents in Nigerian oil and gas projects than their global peers. The fatality per MBOE is 
0.027, which is 2 times that of the U.S. oil and gas industry and 14 times that of the UK oil and gas 
industry. 5: The environmental issue in Nigeria was found to be much worse than in the U.S. and UK by 
analyzing oil spillage data. The Nigerian oil spillage ratio of 18.51 is almost 3.6 times higher than the U.S. 
ratio of 4.00 and 7.2 times higher than the UK ratio of 2.24. The subsurface complexity of the Nigeria 
Delta system, new technology application, local content, community issues, security issues, and 
partnership format were identified as potential factors to explain the project performance. Based on the 
analysis of the subsurface characteristics, the Nigeria Delta system tends to have excellent reservoir 
quality and fluid properties and has high success rate of drilling wells. Because of the excellent reservoir
